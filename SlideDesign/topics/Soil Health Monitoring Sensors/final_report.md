FDR measures soil moisture by sending electromagnetic waves through the soil and analyzing the reflected signals. This technique allows for accurate moisture readings and is more flexible and cost-effective compared to Time Domain Reflectometry (TDR) [1][5].\n\nFDR sensors operate by using the soil as a capacitor, measuring the maximum resonant frequency in the electrical circuit. This frequency correlates with the soil's moisture content, providing real-time data essential for agricultural practices [2]. However, FDR sensors require calibration specific to the soil type to ensure accuracy [1][4]. \n\nIn addition to moisture, FDR technology can also monitor soil temperature, enhancing its utility in precision agriculture [4]. Overall, the integration of FDR in soil health monitoring represents a significant advancement in agricultural technology, enabling better resource management and crop yield optimization.\n\n\n\n![A soil moisture sensor probe in use, demonstrating the application of Frequency Domain Reflectometry (FDR) technology.](https://soilsensor.com/wp-content/uploads/fdr-featured-image.jpg)\n*A soil moisture sensor probe in use, demonstrating the application of Frequency Domain Reflectometry (FDR) technology.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Time-domain and Frequency-domain Reflectometry Type Soil Moisture Sensor Performance and Soil Temperature Effects in Fine- and Coarse-textured Soils: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/332642707_Time-domain_and_Frequency-domain_Reflectometry_Type_Soil_Moisture_Sensor_Performance_and_Soil_Temperature_Effects_in_Fine-_and_Coarse-textured_Soils  \n[2] Soil moisture sensors—How they work.
