When electromagnetic radiation strikes these nanostructures, it induces collective oscillations of conduction electrons, known as plasmons. This phenomenon is particularly pronounced at optical frequencies, where metals typically have a negative dielectric constant, leading to high reflectivity and the ability to sustain surface plasmons at the interface between a metal and a dielectric medium [1][2].\n\nThe behavior of these surface plasmons is crucial for applications in sensors, catalysts, and photothermal devices. The resonance frequencies of plasmons depend on the geometry of the nanostructures, allowing for tailored optical responses. For instance, localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) occurs in small particles, enhancing the optical fields in their vicinity, which can be exploited for various technological applications [3][4]. The primary methods include lithography, chemical synthesis, and self-assembly.\n\nLithography techniques, such as electron beam lithography (EBL) and nanoimprint lithography, allow for precise control over the size and shape of nanostructures. These methods are particularly useful for creating complex patterns on substrates, but they can be time-consuming and costly [2]. In contrast, chemical synthesis methods, including the reduction of metal salts, enable the production of nanoparticles with specific sizes and shapes through simpler processes [5]. \n\nSelf-assembly techniques, such as block copolymer self-assembly, utilize the natural tendency of molecules to organize into structured patterns. This method can produce large areas of nanostructures efficiently [2]. Additionally, a combination of soft lithography and nanosphere lithography has emerged as a promising approach, allowing for the fabrication of flexible and tunable plasmonic nanostructures [4].\n\nOverall, the choice of fabrication technique depends on the desired properties and applications of the plasmonic nanostructures.\n\n\n\n![A multi-step process for fabricating plasmonic nanostructures, including metal deposition and reactive ion etching.](https://pub.mdpi-res.com/biosensors/biosensors-13-00534/article_deploy/html/images/biosensors-13-00534-g001.png?1684396212)\n*A multi-step process for fabricating plasmonic nanostructures, including metal deposition and reactive ion etching.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Plasmonic Nanostructures: Fabrication and Applications: https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/plasmonic-nanostructures-fabrication-applications  \n[2] Harnessing chirality in plasmonics: from synthesis to cutting-edge applications: https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2025/nr/d4nr05229c  \n[3] Fabrication and Characterization of Flexible and Tunable Plasmonic Nanostructures: https://www.nature.com/articles/srep03396  \n[4] Fabrication of plasmonic nanostructures by hole-mask: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2352940718307194  \n[5] NanoPlasMeta 2025 Abstracts: https://photoptics.scitevents.org/Abstract.aspx?idEvent=aKadzKmTSD8=\n\n## Applications in Sensing and Imaging\n\nPlasmonic nanostructures have emerged as pivotal tools in the fields of sensing and imaging, particularly in biosensing and medical diagnostics. These nanostructures exploit surface plasmon resonance (SPR) to enhance the sensitivity of detection methods.
