Key technologies include automated weighing systems, imaging solutions, and animal sensing systems. Automated weighing systems, such as step-on scales and camera-based solutions, provide accurate weight measurements, which are crucial for monitoring animal health and productivity. Imaging solutions, including thermal and 3D cameras, allow for the assessment of animal behavior and physiological conditions, enabling farmers to detect issues like lameness or respiratory problems early on.\n\nAnimal sensing systems, which utilize accelerometers and temperature sensors, are integral to creating an Internet of Things (IoT) environment on farms. These sensors monitor animal health and behavior in real-time, facilitating timely interventions. Additionally, GPS tracking systems enhance management in extensive grazing operations by monitoring animal movements and optimizing grazing patterns. One significant barrier is the high initial investment required for technology adoption, which can create a digital divide between large and small farms. Smaller operations may struggle to access the benefits of PLF, potentially leading to disparities in animal welfare and productivity outcomes across different farm sizes [1][2].\n\nData management issues also pose a challenge. PLF systems generate vast amounts of data, which can overwhelm farmers who may lack the technical expertise to interpret it effectively. This reliance on technology can diminish the traditional farmer-animal relationship, as the focus shifts from personal care to data-driven management [1][3]. Furthermore, ethical concerns arise regarding data privacy and ownership, as farmers may lose control over their operational data to technology providers [2][4].\n\nLastly, the potential for over-reliance on automated systems raises questions about animal welfare. If farmers depend too heavily on technology, they may overlook the nuanced needs of their livestock, leading to negative welfare outcomes [5].
