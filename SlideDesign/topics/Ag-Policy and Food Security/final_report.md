The Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA) of 1933 marked a pivotal shift, aiming to stabilize crop prices by regulating supply through production limits. This policy was a response to the Great Depression, which had devastated farming communities due to surplus production and plummeting prices. By incentivizing farmers to reduce output, the AAA successfully raised crop values, although it also led to higher consumer prices, making food less accessible for many Americans [1][4].\n\nThe 1970s introduced a contrasting approach under Secretary of Agriculture Earl Butz, who promoted overproduction to lower food costs. This shift resulted in a surplus-driven agricultural system, favoring large-scale operations and contributing to the decline of small farms [2][4]. The Food Security Act of 1985 further entrenched government subsidies, creating a complex landscape where certain crops were heavily supported, while others, like fruits and vegetables, received little assistance [2][3]. It introduces eco-schemes that reward farmers for environmentally friendly practices, thus aligning agricultural productivity with ecological goals[1][2].\n\nHowever, recent adjustments to the CAP, prompted by the war in Ukraine, have raised concerns. Political instability is a significant barrier, as it disrupts food production and distribution systems. Conflicts and unrest can lead to the destruction of agricultural infrastructure and displace farming communities, exacerbating food shortages and insecurity [1][5]. Economic constraints, such as inflation and unemployment, further weaken food security by reducing people's purchasing power and access to nutritious food [5]. \n\nEnvironmental factors, including climate change, also pose a serious threat. Extreme weather events can devastate crops and disrupt supply chains, leading to increased food prices and scarcity [2][3]. Additionally, the rapid urbanization process often results in a decline in available agricultural land, further complicating efforts to maintain food production levels [1]. \n\nTo address these challenges, comprehensive policy reforms are necessary.
