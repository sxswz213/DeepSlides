Future efforts should focus on improving data quality and expanding computational resources to overcome existing challenges.\n\n## Fundamentals of Computational Drug Design\n\nComputational drug design is a pivotal aspect of modern pharmaceutical research, primarily categorized into two approaches: structure-based drug design (SBDD) and ligand-based drug design (LBDD). SBDD relies on the three-dimensional (3D) structure of target proteins, often obtained through techniques like X-ray crystallography or predicted using computational methods such as homology modeling. This approach allows researchers to predict how potential drug molecules, or ligands, interact with their targets, estimating binding affinities and optimizing lead compounds based on structural data.\n\nConversely, LBDD does not require detailed structural information about the target. Instead, it infers binding characteristics from known active compounds, making it particularly useful in early drug discovery stages when target structures may be unavailable. Techniques such as quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) modeling and similarity-based virtual screening are commonly employed in LBDD to identify promising candidates from large compound libraries.\n\nThe integration of SBDD and LBDD enhances the drug discovery process, allowing for a more comprehensive understanding of drug-target interactions and improving the efficiency of identifying and optimizing new therapeutic candidates.\n\n\n\n![Comparison of structure-based and ligand-based drug design approaches.](https://image.slidesharecdn.com/structurebasedandligandbaseddrugdesigning-180927113948/85/Structure-based-and-ligand-based-drug-designing-4-320.jpg)\n*Comparison of structure-based and ligand-based drug design approaches.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Leveraging ligand-based and structure-based approaches for advancing drug discovery: https://www.iptonline.com/articles/leveraging-ligand-based-and-structure-based-approaches-for-advan  \n[2] Computational approaches to structure-based ligand design: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0163725899000315  \n[3] Computer-Aided Drug Design Methods - PMC - PubMed Central: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5248982/  \n[4] Computational Methods in Drug Discovery - PMC - PubMed Central: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3880464/  \n[5] Drug Design: Structure- and Ligand-Based Approaches - Amazon.com: https://www.amazon.com/Drug-Design-Structure-Ligand-Based-Approaches/dp/0521887232\n\n## Key Techniques in Computational Drug Design\n\nComputational drug design employs various techniques to predict how drugs interact with biological targets. One of the most prominent methods is **molecular docking**, which simulates the binding of small molecules (ligands) to their target proteins. This technique allows researchers to predict the preferred orientation of a ligand and estimate its binding affinity, which is crucial for drug development [1][3]. Molecular docking can be performed using different approaches, including rigid and flexible docking, with the latter accounting for the dynamic nature of proteins [2][4].\n\nAnother essential technique is **Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) modeling**, which establishes mathematical relationships between the chemical structure of compounds and their biological activity. This method helps in predicting the efficacy of new compounds based on known data [5]. Additionally, **pharmacophore modeling** identifies the essential features of a molecule required for biological activity, guiding the design of new drugs that fit these criteria [5].\n\nThese techniques, combined with advancements in machine learning and artificial intelligence, are transforming the landscape of drug discovery, making it faster and more efficient [2][5].\n\n\n\n![Illustration of the molecular docking process in drug design, showcasing the identification of drug target proteins and evaluation of binding
