A key characteristic of fractals is **self-similarity**, which means that a fractal appears similar at different scales. For instance, zooming into a fractal reveals patterns that resemble the overall shape, a property evident in natural forms like coastlines and ferns [2][3]. \n\nFractals are defined mathematically by their **fractal dimension**, which often exceeds their topological dimension. This dimension quantifies how a fractal scales compared to traditional geometric shapes. For example, while a line is one-dimensional and a square is two-dimensional, a fractal can have a dimension that is a non-integer value, indicating its complexity [1][5]. \n\nThe most famous examples of fractals include the **Mandelbrot set** and the **Sierpinski triangle**. The Sierpinski triangle is created by recursively removing triangles from a larger triangle, demonstrating both self-similarity and the concept of fractal dimension [4][5]. \n\nUnderstanding these fundamental concepts is crucial for exploring the applications of fractal geometry in various fields, including nature, art, and science.\n\n\n\n![A large Sierpinski triangle displaying self-similarity through nested smaller triangles.](https://graphicmaths.com/img/fractals/introduction/fractal-dimension-self-similar/sierpinski2.png)\n*A large Sierpinski triangle displaying self-similarity through nested smaller triangles.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Self-Similarity and Fractal Dimension - Blake Mellor: https://blakemellor.lmu.build/courses/Symmetry/FractalDimension-Spring2013.pdf  \n[2] Fractal Basics | Mathematics for the Liberal Arts - Lumen Learning: https://courses.lumenlearning.com/wmopen-mathforliberalarts/chapter/introduction-fractal-basics/  \n[3] Introduction to Fractals and Self-Similarity - Chaos Theory - Fiveable: https://library.fiveable.me/chaos-theory/unit-5/introduction-fractals-self-similarity/study-guide/3dekHQHi2027nqRA  \n[4] Fractals: Mandelbrot - Science On a Sphere: https://sos.noaa.gov/catalog/datasets/fractals-mandelbrot/  \n[5] Fractal - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal\n\n## Applications in Nature\n\nFractal geometry is prevalent in various natural phenomena, showcasing self-similar patterns across different scales. This hypothesis suggests that different types of investors operate on varying time horizons, which influences market behavior and volatility. For instance, short-term traders may react quickly to market news, while long-term investors focus on fundamental values. This divergence can lead to market inefficiencies, particularly during periods of heightened uncertainty, where a convergence of investor behavior towards short-term strategies can exacerbate volatility and liquidity issues.\n\nDeveloped by Ed Peters in 1994, the FMH challenges the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) by asserting that markets are not always efficient and that investor behavior is influenced by psychological factors and varying information sets. During turbulent market conditions, such as the Global Financial Crisis, the FMH predicts a dominance of short-term investment horizons, which can lead to extreme price fluctuations and market crashes. This perspective provides a more nuanced understanding of market dynamics compared to traditional models, emphasizing the importance of liquidity and the role of diverse investor strategies in maintaining market stability.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of the fractal markets hypothesis, showing a market trend with nested fractal structures.](https://i.ytimg.com/vi/gwU_2FNXPFo/maxresdefault.jpg)\n*Illustration of the fractal markets hypothesis, showing a market trend with nested fractal structures.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Fractal Market Hypothesis: An In-Depth Review - SSRN: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/Delivery.cfm/5137493.pdf?abstractid=5137493&mirid=1  \n[2] Fractal Markets Hypothesis (FMH): What it is, How it Works - Investopedia: https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/fractal-markets-hypothesis-fmh.asp  \n[3] Fractal Markets Hypothesis and the Global Financial Crisis - Nature: https://www.nature.com/articles/srep02857  \n[4] The Fractal Market Hypothesis: Applications to Financial Forecasting - Technological University Dublin: https://arrow.tudublin.ie/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1010&context=engschelebk  \n[5] Fractal Market Hypothesis: An In-Depth Review - SSRN: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5137493\n\n# Fractal Geometry Applications\n\nFractal geometry is a fascinating field that explores complex shapes and patterns found in nature, technology, art, and finance.
