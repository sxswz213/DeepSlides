Notable cases such as *Vedanta Resources Plc v. Lungowe* and *Okpabi v. Royal Dutch Shell* have established that parent companies can be held liable for the actions of their subsidiaries, particularly when they exert substantial control over their operations. The UK Supreme Court's rulings in these cases indicate a growing willingness to recognize a duty of care owed by parent companies to third parties affected by their subsidiaries' activities, thereby expanding the scope of corporate liability in ESG matters.\n\nIn *Maran v. Begum*, the court further clarified these principles, emphasizing that parent companies must actively manage and oversee their subsidiaries' compliance with ESG standards. This trend reflects a broader shift towards holding corporations accountable for their global operations, particularly in jurisdictions where they may be perceived as more favorable for claimants. The recent adoption of the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) by the EU introduces stringent due diligence obligations, reinforcing corporate accountability in ESG compliance and urging companies to enhance their governance frameworks to mitigate potential legal risks.\n\n\n\n![A woman gestures towards charts and graphs on computer screens, illustrating discussions on ESG risk and compliance trends.](https://www.auditxcellence.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/esg-risk-compliance-current-and-future-trends.jpg)\n*A woman gestures towards charts and graphs on computer screens, illustrating discussions on ESG risk and compliance trends.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Corporate Group Liability and ESG -
