It encompasses a wide range of activities, including disaster planning, response, recovery, and mitigation efforts. The primary goal is to ensure that health services are operational before, during, and after disasters, thereby minimizing the negative health impacts on affected populations.\n\nThe phases of disaster management include prevention, which involves strategies to avert disasters; mitigation, aimed at reducing the severity of disasters; response, which is the immediate action taken to address the needs of affected individuals; and recovery, focusing on restoring health services and infrastructure. Their responsibilities include risk communication, incident management, and ensuring continuity of care. Public health departments are often the first responders, providing essential information to communities about health risks and safety measures during disasters. They also conduct needs assessments to identify vulnerable populations and prioritize resources effectively [1][4].\n\nCoordination among various health organizations is vital for an effective response. The Incident Command System (ICS) is commonly employed to streamline communication and resource allocation among agencies, ensuring a unified approach to disaster management [2][3]. This system allows for rapid mobilization of healthcare resources and personnel, facilitating timely medical care and support for affected individuals.\n\nMoreover, collaboration between public health and healthcare systems enhances preparedness and response capabilities.
