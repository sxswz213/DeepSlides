This classification includes diseases that are newly recognized, newly introduced, or evolved due to various factors such as environmental changes, human demographics, and pathogen adaptations [2][4]. \n\nEmerging diseases can be categorized into several types: newly emerging diseases, re-emerging diseases, new manifestations of known agents, and the introduction of known agents to new territories [3][5]. Notable examples include HIV, Ebola, and Zika virus, which have all posed significant public health challenges in recent decades. The rise of these diseases is often linked to human activities, such as urbanization and climate change, which facilitate the spread of pathogens from animals to humans [1][3].\n\nUnderstanding the classification and dynamics of emerging diseases is crucial for developing effective surveillance and response strategies to mitigate their impact on global health.\n\n\n\n![Classification of emerging infectious diseases, including newly emerging, reemerging, and deliberately emerging categories.](http://image.slideserve.com/717192/classification-of-emerging-infectious-diseases-l.jpg)\n*Classification of emerging infectious diseases, including newly emerging, reemerging, and deliberately emerging categories.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Emerging Disease or Emerging Diagnosis? - https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Yfk-B7cftik  \n[2] Emerging diseases | Health topics - https://www.emro.who.int/health-topics/emerging-diseases/index.html  \n[3] Emerging infectious disease - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emerging_infectious_disease  \n[4] Emerging Infectious Disease - an overview - https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/emerging-infectious-disease  \n[5] Global Biothreats and Emerging Infectious Diseases - https://med.virginia.edu/infectious-diseases/research-programs/global-biothreats-and-emerging-infectious-diseases/\n\n## Factors Contributing to the Emergence of Diseases\n\nThe emergence of infectious diseases is influenced by a complex interplay of environmental, social, and biological factors. Rapid global population growth and urbanization have increased human contact with pathogens, facilitating disease transmission. As people migrate to urban areas, overcrowding and inadequate sanitation create ideal conditions for outbreaks of diseases like tuberculosis and cholera [1][3].\n\nEnvironmental changes, including climate change and habitat destruction, also play a significant role. These alterations can expand the range of vectors such as mosquitoes, leading to the spread of diseases like malaria and dengue fever [2][4]. Additionally, human behaviors, such as increased international travel and trade, contribute to the rapid dissemination of pathogens across borders [3][5].\n\nBiological factors, including microbial evolution and the emergence of antibiotic resistance, further complicate the landscape of infectious diseases. Pathogens adapt to new environments and hosts, making them more virulent and difficult to control [1][2]. Understanding these interconnected factors is crucial for developing effective strategies to prevent and respond to emerging infectious diseases.\n\n\n\n![Factors involved in the emergence of infectious diseases.](http://image.slideserve.com/717192/factors-involved-in-the-emergence-of-infectious-diseases6-l.jpg)\n*Factors involved in the emergence of infectious diseases.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Major factors affecting the emergence and re-emergence of infectious diseases: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7119055/  \n[2] What causes outbreaks? - Infectious Disease Emergencies: https://emergencies.pubpub.org/pub/what-causes-outbreaks  \n[3] Implications of human activities for (re)emerging infectious diseases: https://jphysiolanthropol.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s40101-020-00239-5  \n[4] Environmental and social influences on emerging infectious diseases: https://academic.oup.com/book/7131/chapter/151669203  \n[5] The Rise of Environmental Influences on Infectious Diseases: https://journals.lww.com/epidem/fulltext/2008/11001/the_rise_of_environmental_influences_on_infectious.91.aspx\n\n## Epidemiological Trends and Patterns\n\nEmerging infectious diseases (EIDs) have shown a significant increase in incidence and geographic spread over the past few decades. A comprehensive analysis of global outbreaks from 1996 to 2023 revealed 3,013 reported events, with respiratory infections, particularly influenza, being the most frequent, followed by Ebola and MERS-CoV outbreaks. The Democratic Republic of the Congo, China, and Saudi Arabia were identified as hotspots for these outbreaks, highlighting the need for targeted public health interventions in these regions [1].\n\nThe trends indicate that EIDs are increasingly concentrated in low-latitude developing countries, where socioeconomic factors such as population density and economic development play a crucial role in disease emergence [2]. Climate change further exacerbates these trends, influencing the distribution and transmission dynamics of pathogens [3]. For instance, vector-borne diseases like dengue and malaria are becoming more prevalent in regions previously unaffected, driven by changing environmental conditions [4]. \n\nUnderstanding these epidemiological patterns is essential for developing effective surveillance and response strategies to mitigate the impact of future outbreaks.\n\n\n\n![High incidence rates of emerging diseases in the Caribbean and parts of Africa, with some regions reporting over 100 cases per 100,000 people in 2023.](https://www.ecdc.europa.eu/sites/default/files/images/dengue-june-2022-may-2023.png)\n*High incidence rates of emerging diseases in the Caribbean and parts of Africa, with some regions reporting over 100 cases per 100,000 people in 2023.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Global distribution and health impact of infectious disease outbreaks, 1996–2023: a worldwide retrospective analysis of World Health Organization emergency event reports - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12082254/  \n[2] Impact of dual climatic and socioeconomic factors on global trends in infectious disease outbreaks: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-83431-2  \n[3] Emerging and reemerging infectious diseases: global trends and implications: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41392-024-01917-x  \n[4] Emerging and Re-Emerging Diseases: https://publichealth.jhu.edu/sites/default/files/2023-10/emerging-diseases-marcoem-conference-presentation-10-27-23-final-2.pdf\n\n## Global Response and Surveillance Strategies\n\nThe global response to emerging diseases relies heavily on coordinated efforts from organizations like the World Health Organization (WHO) and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).
