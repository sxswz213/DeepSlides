One prominent approach is **fidelity criticism**, which evaluates adaptations based on their faithfulness to the original text. While this method can highlight changes made during adaptation, it often privileges the source material, potentially stifling the creative possibilities inherent in film as a medium [1][5].\n\nIn contrast, **intertextuality** emphasizes the interconnectedness of texts, suggesting that adaptations are part of a broader cultural dialogue. This perspective recognizes that films draw from multiple sources, including other films, literature, and cultural contexts, creating a dynamic interpretive exchange [2][5]. Furthermore, the concept of **adaptation as interpretation** posits that filmmakers reimagine source material through their unique creative lenses, resulting in distinct narratives that reflect their vision and the constraints of the cinematic form [1][5]. \n\nThese frameworks collectively illustrate that adaptations are not mere reproductions but complex reinterpretations that engage with and transform their source materials, enriching both the original and the adapted works. One of the primary hurdles is condensing the often sprawling narratives of novels into a limited runtime. Novels can explore intricate plots and character development over hundreds of pages, while films typically have a duration of about two hours. This necessitates the careful selection of key scenes and themes, often leading to the omission of subplots and secondary characters, which can dilute the story's richness and emotional depth [1][3][5].\n\nAnother significant challenge is translating the internal experiences of characters into a visual medium. Novels frequently delve into characters' thoughts and emotions, providing a depth that films must externalize through dialogue and performance. This can result in oversimplification, where the complexity of character arcs is lost [2][4]. Additionally, filmmakers must balance fidelity to the source material with the creative liberties required for cinematic storytelling. Deviating too far from the original can alienate dedicated fans, while strict adherence may lead to a cluttered narrative [3][5].\n\nWhen adapting plays, filmmakers face unique challenges such as maintaining engagement in static scenes and avoiding over-theatrical performances. The transition from stage to screen requires a shift in narrative approach, emphasizing visual storytelling over dialogue. Familiarity with the source material significantly shapes viewers' expectations and interpretations. According to reception theory, audiences bring their own cultural contexts and personal experiences to the viewing experience, influencing how they engage with the film. This \"horizon of expectations\" can lead to varied interpretations, as seen in adaptations like Baz Luhrmann's *The Great Gatsby*, where some viewers appreciated the modern aesthetic while others felt it detracted from Fitzgerald's original prose [1][2].\n\nAdaptations often serve as dialogues between the original text and contemporary audiences, allowing filmmakers to reinterpret narratives to resonate with modern values and concerns.
