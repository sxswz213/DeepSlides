It encompasses the application of veterinary science to protect and improve human well-being, particularly through the management of zoonotic diseases—diseases that can be transmitted from animals to humans. The World Health Organization (WHO) defines veterinary public health as the sum of all contributions to the complete physical, mental, and social well-being of humans through veterinary medical science[1](https://www.who.int/teams/control-of-neglected-tropical-diseases/interventions/strategies/veterinary-public-health).\n\nThe scope of veterinary public health includes disease prevention, control, and surveillance, emphasizing the need for a collaborative approach among veterinary and human health professionals. This multidisciplinary effort is crucial for addressing emerging zoonotic diseases, which pose significant threats to global health. For instance, approximately 75% of newly emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic, highlighting the importance of integrated health strategies[4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1289303/). Approximately 60% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonoses, with notable examples including SARS, West Nile virus, and avian influenza. These diseases can spread through various pathways, such as direct contact with infected animals, consumption of contaminated food, or through vectors like mosquitoes and ticks. \n\nThe One Health approach emphasizes the interconnectedness of human, animal, and environmental health, advocating for collaborative efforts to monitor and control zoonotic diseases. Effective prevention strategies include enhancing surveillance systems, improving laboratory diagnostics, and fostering inter-sectoral collaboration among health professionals. \n\nThe economic impact of zoonotic diseases is profound, affecting trade, tourism, and livestock productivity. For instance, the emergence of SARS in 2003 resulted in an estimated global economic loss of over $50 billion. Addressing these public health threats requires a unified response that integrates veterinary and public health efforts to mitigate risks and enhance community resilience.\n\n\n\n![Connections between sources of emerging zoonotic diseases, including live animal markets and wildlife hunting.](https://www.frontiersin.org/files/Articles/582743/fvets-07-582743-HTML/image_m/fvets-07-582743-g001.jpg)\n*Connections between sources of emerging zoonotic diseases, including live animal markets and wildlife hunting.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] About One Health - CDC: https://www.cdc.gov/one-health/about/index.html  \n[2] Zoonotic disease: emerging public health threats in the Region | RC61: https://www.emro.who.int/about-who/rc61/zoonotic-diseases.html  \n[3] Emerging Zoonotic Diseases: Epidemiology, Public Health Impact ...: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389383263_Emerging_Zoonotic_Diseases_Epidemiology_Public_Health_Impact_and_the_Urgent_Need_for_a_Unified_One_Health_Approach  \n[4] Zoonotic Diseases: Etiology, Impact, and Control - PubMed Central: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7563794/  \n[5] Occupational zoonoses, neurological diseases, and public health: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2772431X25000231\n\n## One Health Approach in Veterinary Public Health\n\nThe One Health approach recognizes the interconnectedness of human, animal, and environmental health. This collaborative framework is essential for addressing complex health challenges, particularly zoonotic diseases that can spread between species. By integrating expertise from various sectors, including veterinary medicine, public health, and environmental science, One Health aims to improve health outcomes for all.\n\nOne Health initiatives focus on monitoring and controlling health threats at the interface of humans, animals, and ecosystems. For instance, surveillance systems that track diseases in animal populations can provide early warnings of potential outbreaks affecting humans. This proactive strategy not only helps in preventing zoonotic diseases but also addresses broader issues like antimicrobial resistance and food safety. \n\nThe success of the One Health approach relies on effective communication and collaboration among diverse stakeholders, including healthcare providers, veterinarians, and policymakers. By fostering partnerships, One Health enhances our ability to predict, prevent, and respond to health threats, ultimately promoting a healthier environment for all.\n\n\n\n![The One Health Triad illustrates the interdependence of healthy people, animals, and environments.](https://www.publichealthnotes.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/11/One_Health_Triad.jpg)\n*The One Health Triad illustrates the interdependence of healthy people, animals, and environments.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Working Together for One Health: https://www.cdc.gov/one-health/php/stories/working-together-for-one-health.html  \n[2] About One Health: https://www.cdc.gov/one-health/about/index.html  \n[3] One Health: https://www.avma.org/resources-tools/one-health  \n[4] A Holistic Approach to Tackling Global Health Issues - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11042131/  \n[5] Facilitating implementation of the one health approach: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9900431/\n\n## Implementation Strategies and Challenges\n\nImplementing veterinary public health policies requires multifaceted strategies, particularly in developing countries. Community-based approaches are essential, as they engage local populations in livestock management and animal health activities, fostering ownership and sustainability. Training programs for veterinary professionals are also critical, ensuring they are equipped with the necessary skills to address public health challenges effectively.
