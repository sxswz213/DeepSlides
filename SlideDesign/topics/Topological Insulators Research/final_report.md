In these materials, the bulk is insulating, but conductive states exist on the surface, protected by topological invariants. The band structure of a topological insulator typically features an energy gap between the conduction and valence bands, with surface states that connect these bands.\n\nTime-reversal symmetry plays a crucial role in the behavior of topological insulators. It ensures that for every spin-up state at momentum \\( k \\), there is a corresponding spin-down state at momentum \\( -k \\), leading to the formation of helical edge states that are robust against non-magnetic impurities. Spin-orbit coupling is essential for creating the band inversion necessary for these topological phases, allowing the conduction and valence bands to swap their order, which is a hallmark of topological insulators.\n\nThe interplay of these factors results in materials that can conduct electricity on their surfaces while remaining insulating in the bulk, making them promising for various applications in electronics and quantum computing.\n\n\n\n![Band structure of a topological insulator showing the conduction and valence bands, along with the surface states.](https://handwiki.org/wiki/images/thumb/f/fd/Topological_insulator_band_structure.svg/1200px-Topological_insulator_band_structure.svg.png)\n*Band structure of a topological insulator showing the conduction and valence bands, along with the surface states.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Engineering three-dimensional topological insulators in Rashba ...: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3709477/  \n[2] How Fundamental is Spin-Orbit Coupling to Topological Insulators?: https://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/26885/how-fundamental-is-spin-orbit-coupling-to-topological-insulators  \n[3] Topological insulators | Solid State Physics Class Notes - Fiveable: https://library.fiveable.me/solid-state-physics/unit-12/topological-insulators/study-guide/S2lu6AgxPbm7BQ8v  \n[4] Topological insulators with inversion symmetry | Phys. The two primary types are two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) topological insulators. \n\n2D topological insulators, such as the quantum spin Hall insulator, exhibit conducting edge states while maintaining an insulating bulk. These edge states are protected by time-reversal symmetry, allowing for robust transport properties even in the presence of disorder. In contrast, 3D topological insulators, like Bi2Se3, feature surface states that are also topologically protected, leading to unique electronic properties and potential applications in spintronics and quantum computing.\n\nAnother significant category is magnetic topological insulators, which incorporate magnetic elements into their structure. These materials can exhibit phenomena such as the quantum anomalous Hall effect, where edge states become chiral due to the presence of magnetism. Lastly, higher-order topological insulators represent a more recent classification, where the boundary states exist not only on the surface but also on the edges of the material, leading to novel physical phenomena.\n\n### Image Selection\n\n\n\n![Comparison of electronic band structures and edge states in insulating and quantum Hall states.](https://image3.slideserve.com/6572283/topological-inequivalence-of-insulators-l.jpg)\n*Comparison of electronic band structures and edge states in insulating and quantum Hall states.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Topological classification of higher-order topological phases with ...: https://link.aps.org/doi/10.1103/PhysRevB.106.245105  \n[2] Higher-order topological insulators - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5983919/  \n[3] Higher-order topological insulators | Science Advances: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.aat0346  \n[4] 10 symmetry classes and the periodic table of topological insulators: https://topocondmat.org/w8_general/classification.html  \n[5] Recent advances in 2D, 3D and higher-order topological photonics: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41377-020-0331-y\n\n## Applications of Topological Insulators\n\nTopological insulators (TIs) are emerging as pivotal materials in the fields of spintronics and quantum computing.
