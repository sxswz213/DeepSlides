By enabling targeted delivery and controlled release of drugs, these innovative systems address the limitations of traditional therapies. The ongoing development in this field holds promise for revolutionizing therapeutic strategies and improving patient outcomes.\n\n## Types of Nanocarriers\n\nNanocarriers are essential in nano-medicine, facilitating the targeted delivery of drugs. They can be categorized into several types, each with unique structures and mechanisms. \n\n**Liposomes** are spherical vesicles composed of phospholipid bilayers, capable of encapsulating both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs. Their biocompatibility makes them suitable for various therapeutic applications, although they may face stability issues in circulation [1][3].\n\n**Dendrimers** are highly branched, tree-like polymers that allow for precise drug attachment and controlled release. Their customizable structure enhances drug delivery efficiency, making them versatile carriers in therapeutic applications [2][3].\n\n**Polymeric nanoparticles** are formed from biodegradable polymers, providing a stable environment for drug encapsulation. They can be engineered to release drugs in a controlled manner, improving therapeutic outcomes [1][4].\n\n**Carbon nanotubes** are cylindrical nanostructures with exceptional mechanical and electrical properties. Their hollow structure allows for drug loading, and they can penetrate cell membranes effectively, making them promising candidates for drug delivery systems [3][4].\n\nThese nanocarriers enhance drug solubility, stability, and targeted delivery, addressing the limitations of conventional drug delivery methods.\n\n\n\n![Schematic representation of different types of nanocarriers used in drug delivery.](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Monica-Alves/publication/331437049/figure/fig3/AS:1151992986648588@1651667939603/Schematic-representation-of-different-types-of-nanocarriers-used-in-drug-delivery.png)\n*Schematic representation of different types of nanocarriers used in drug delivery.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Nano-based drug delivery systems: Conventional ... - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S259009862200015X  \n[2] An Overview of Polymeric Nanoparticles-Based Drug ... - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9893377/  \n[3] Nanocarriers in Drug Delivery Systems: An Overview - https://sciensage.info/index.php/JASR/article/download/2397/1686  \n[4] Nanocarrier - an overview - https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/agricultural-and-biological-sciences/nanocarrier\n\n## Mechanisms of Drug Delivery\n\nNanocarriers utilize various mechanisms to deliver drugs effectively, primarily through passive and active targeting strategies. Passive targeting leverages the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, which allows nanoparticles to accumulate in tumor tissues due to their leaky vasculature. This method relies on the size and properties of the nanocarriers, enabling them to navigate through biological barriers and reach the target site without specific modifications [[1]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2790676025000147) [[3]](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jphp.13098).\n\nIn contrast, active targeting involves the functionalization of nanocarriers with ligands that bind to specific receptors on target cells. This approach enhances the selectivity of drug delivery, minimizing effects on healthy tissues and improving therapeutic outcomes [[2]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40383609/) [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9893377/). Additionally, controlled release mechanisms can be integrated into nanocarriers, allowing for the timed release of drugs in response to specific stimuli, such as pH or temperature changes [[1]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2790676025000147).\n\nSurface modifications of nanocarriers play a crucial role in enhancing delivery efficiency by improving stability, circulation time, and cellular uptake [[2]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40383609/). Overall, the combination of these mechanisms significantly advances the efficacy of nanomedicine in treating various diseases.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of nanocarriers navigating blood vessels with surface modifications for passive tissue targeting and mechanisms for active cellular targeting.](https://ebrary.net/htm/img/14/1140/8.png)\n*Illustration of nanocarriers navigating blood vessels with surface modifications for passive tissue targeting and mechanisms for active cellular targeting.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Nanoparticles for drug delivery: Insight into in vitro and in vivo drug release from nanomedicines - ScienceDirect: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2790676025000147  \n[2] Advances in nanocarriers for targeted drug delivery and controlled drug release: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40383609/  \n[3] An overview of active and passive targeting strategies to improve the nanocarriers efficiency to tumour sites: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jphp.13098  \n[4] An Overview of Polymeric Nanoparticles-Based Drug Delivery Systems: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9893377/\n\n## Applications in Disease Treatment\n\nNanomedicine has emerged as a transformative approach in treating various diseases, particularly cancer. This is primarily accomplished through the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, which allows nanoparticles to accumulate preferentially in tumor tissues due to their leaky vasculature. Additionally, active targeting strategies involve functionalizing nanoparticles with ligands that bind specifically to tumor cells, further increasing the precision of drug delivery [1][2][3].\n\nRecent advancements in nanotechnology have led to the development of multifunctional nanoparticles that can serve both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes, paving the way for theranostic applications.
