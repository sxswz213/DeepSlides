Future efforts should focus on integrating multilingual education strategies to support diverse linguistic communities.\n\n## Definitions of Language Policy and Planning\n\nLanguage policy and planning (LPP) are essential for managing linguistic diversity in societies. Language policy refers to the ideological goals and principles that dictate how languages are utilized and regulated within a community. It includes decisions made by governments or institutions about which languages to promote, preserve, or restrict, reflecting broader social, political, and cultural objectives. For example, a bilingual policy may be adopted to support both majority and minority languages, fostering inclusivity and cultural identity.\n\nConversely, language planning involves the concrete actions and strategies implemented to achieve the goals set by language policy. This includes developing educational programs, creating language resources, and instituting regulations affecting language use in public life. Effective language planning is crucial for addressing communication challenges among diverse linguistic groups and ensuring that language policies are successfully enacted. Together, language policy and planning shape the linguistic landscape of a society, influencing education, social cohesion, and cultural identity.\n\n\n\n![Comparison of language planning as an activity promoting systematic change and language policy as a body of ideas and regulations.](https://image.slidesharecdn.com/languageplanningandpolicy-200103102815/85/Language-Planning-and-Policy-3-320.jpg)\n*Comparison of language planning as an activity promoting systematic change and language policy as a body of ideas and regulations.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Language planning and language policy (Chapter 20) - Cambridge University Press: https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/cambridge-handbook-of-sociolinguistics/language-planning-and-language-policy/4D1B6240BB22A94B3C7934ECA32DDF84  \n[2] 14.3 Language planning and policy - Intro To Linguistics - Fiveable: https://library.fiveable.me/introduction-linguistics/unit-14/language-planning-policy/study-guide/mGIVYumQGvpPZx0m  \n[3] LANGUAGE PLANNING AND LANGUAGE POLICY - University of Biskra: https://elearning.univ-biskra.dz/moodle/mod/resource/view.php?id=165538  \n[4] Language Planning and Policy (LPP) - American Association For Applied Linguistics: https://www.aaal.org/lpp  \n[5] What is the difference between language planning and language policy? - Quora: https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-difference-between-language-planning-and-language-policy\n\n## Types of Language Planning\n\nLanguage planning is a systematic approach to influence the function, structure, or acquisition of languages within a community. It is generally divided into three main types: status planning, corpus planning, and acquisition planning.\n\nStatus planning involves decisions regarding the allocation of languages to various societal functions. This includes designating a language as an official language or determining its use in government, education, and media. For instance, the recognition of Quechua as an official language in Peru in 1975 exemplifies how status planning can enhance a language's prestige and functionality in a multilingual context [1][4].\n\nCorpus planning focuses on the structural aspects of a language, such as grammar, vocabulary, and writing systems. This type of planning aims to standardize and modernize a language to meet contemporary needs. An example is the development of a standardized writing system for Quechua, which faced challenges due to dialectal variations [3][4].\n\nAcquisition planning pertains to educational strategies that promote language learning and usage. This includes curriculum development and teacher training to ensure effective language instruction. Bilingual education programs in Peru, aimed at enhancing the acquisition of both Spanish and Quechua, illustrate this type of planning [4][5].\n\n\n\n![Overview of the three types of language planning: status, corpus, and acquisition.](https://slideplayer.com/slide/14032736/86/images/6/Language+planning+Language+planning+is+constituted+by+three+different+processes,+viz.+status+planning,+corpus+planning+and+acquisition+planning..jpg)\n*Overview of the three types of language planning: status, corpus, and acquisition.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Language planning - (Intro to Humanities) | Fiveable - Fiveable: https://library.fiveable.me/key-terms/introduction-humanities/language-planning  \n[2] Language policy Status Planning | PPTX | Education - SlideShare: https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/language-policy-status-planning/130216465  \n[3] The Relationship Between Language Policy and Language Planning: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/387716675_The_Relationship_Between_Language_Policy_and_Language_Planning_A_Sociolinguistic_Perspective  \n[4] Language planning - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_planning  \n[5] Language Planning Handouts | PDF | Linguistics - Scribd: https://www.scribd.com/document/471589085/Language-Planning-Handouts\n\n## Language Policy in Education\n\nLanguage policy is essential in shaping educational practices, especially in multilingual contexts. Effective language planning enhances educational outcomes by ensuring that students learn in a language they understand. Research shows that when the language of instruction aligns with students' mother tongues, it improves comprehension and retention, leading to better academic performance [1][2].\n\nIn linguistically diverse regions, such as the Asia-Pacific, challenges arise when the language of instruction differs from students' home languages, often resulting in higher dropout rates [2]. Comprehensive language policies that incorporate mother tongue education can bridge the gap between home and school, providing crucial support for students from ethnic minorities [2][4]. \n\nFurthermore, multilingual education fosters social equity by addressing language barriers and creating inclusive learning environments. By valuing linguistic diversity, educational systems can enhance students' sense of belonging, ultimately enriching their educational experiences [4][5].
