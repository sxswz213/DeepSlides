It focuses on understanding how changes in DNA—either inherited (germline) or acquired (somatic)—contribute to the uncontrolled growth of cells. Genomics encompasses the analysis of entire genomes, including mutations, copy number variations, and structural changes, which can provide insights into the mechanisms of cancer progression and treatment responses.\n\nKey terms in cancer genomics include **genomics**, which refers to the comprehensive study of an organism's complete set of DNA, including its genes; **genetic testing**, which involves analyzing DNA to identify mutations that may lead to cancer; and the role of **DNA** in cancer, where specific mutations can drive tumorigenesis. For instance, mutations in genes like BRCA1 and BRCA2 are linked to increased breast and ovarian cancer risk, highlighting the importance of genetic testing in identifying individuals at risk and tailoring preventive measures.\n\nThe integration of genomic data into clinical practice has paved the way for precision medicine, allowing treatments to be tailored based on the specific genetic makeup of a patient's tumor, thereby improving outcomes and minimizing side effects.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of genetic information flow and types of mutations in cancer.](https://www.cancer.gov/sites/g/files/xnrzdm211/files/styles/cgov_article/public/cgov_image/media_image/100/000/7/files/NCI_CCG_Genetics_Infographic_v12_full-article.jpg?itok=kd55zyO8)\n*Illustration of genetic information flow and types of mutations in cancer.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Cancer genome research and precision medicine - NCI: https://www.cancer.gov/ccg/research/cancer-genomics-overview  \n[2] Research Areas: Cancer Genomics - NCI: https://www.cancer.gov/research/areas/genomics  \n[3] The emerging clinical relevance of genomics in cancer medicine: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6658089/  \n[4] Cancer genomics: https://www.genomicseducation.hee.nhs.uk/cancer-genomics/  \n[5] Cancer Genomics - ScienceDirect.com: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0188440922001539\n\n## Types of Genomic Variants in Cancer\n\nGenomic variants in cancer can be classified into two main types: inherited (germline) and acquired (somatic) variants. Germline variants are genetic changes present from birth, passed down from parents to offspring through reproductive cells. These variants can increase the risk of developing certain cancers, such as breast and ovarian cancer, but do not guarantee cancer will occur. Approximately 5-10% of all cancers are attributed to inherited mutations, which can be identified through genetic testing [1][5].\n\nIn contrast, somatic variants arise during a person's lifetime due to environmental factors or cellular processes, such as exposure to carcinogens or errors during cell division. These variants are not inherited and are found only in specific cells, leading to sporadic cancers. Somatic mutations are the most common cause of cancer, accounting for the majority of cases [2][4]. Understanding the distinction between these two types of variants is crucial for assessing cancer risk and tailoring treatment strategies.\n\n\n\n![Comparison of germline and somatic variants in cancer.](https://www.genomicseducation.hee.nhs.uk/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/Germline-vs-somatic.jpg)\n*Comparison of germline and somatic variants in cancer.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Acquired and inherited cancer gene variants: https://www.macmillan.org.uk/cancer-information-and-support/worried-about-cancer/causes-and-risk-factors/genes-and-cancer  \n[2] Germline and Somatic Variants: What Is the Difference?: https://www.ons.org/publications-research/voice/news-views/01-2020/germline-and-somatic-variants-what-difference  \n[3] Cancer Genomics and Inherited Risk - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5795694/  \n[4] Inherited Mutations and Hereditary Cancer: https://www.facingourrisk.org/info/hereditary-cancer-and-genetic-testing/hereditary-cancer/inherited-mutations-and-cancer  \n[5] The Genetics of Cancer - NCI: https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/causes-prevention/genetics\n\n## Clinical Applications of Cancer Genomics\n\nCancer genomics has transformed clinical practice by enabling personalized medicine, which tailors treatment based on individual genetic profiles. This approach enhances therapeutic efficacy and minimizes adverse effects, addressing the complexity of tumor heterogeneity. Advances in next-generation sequencing (NGS) have facilitated the identification of actionable mutations, such as EGFR in non-small cell lung cancer and BRAF in melanoma, leading to the development of targeted therapies that significantly improve patient outcomes [1][2].\n\nRecent studies indicate that genomically-matched treatments can yield up to 85% better patient outcomes, with targeted therapies showing response rates as high as 76% in CAR-T cell therapy for treatment-resistant cancers [2][3]. Furthermore, the integration of biomarkers in treatment selection allows for real-time monitoring of treatment responses, enabling timely adjustments to therapy based on individual patient needs [4][5]. Despite these advancements, challenges remain in data interpretation, equitable access, and the integration of genomic information into routine clinical workflows [1][4].\n\nThe future of cancer care lies in the continued evolution of personalized medicine, supported by interdisciplinary collaboration and technological innovations.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of personalized cancer therapy, highlighting molecular profiling and treatment tailoring based on genetic factors.](https://ufluidix.com/circle/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/Personalized-cancer-therapy.jpg)\n*Illustration of personalized cancer therapy, highlighting molecular profiling and treatment tailoring based on genetic factors.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Advances in personalized medicine: translating genomic insights into targeted therapies for cancer treatment.
