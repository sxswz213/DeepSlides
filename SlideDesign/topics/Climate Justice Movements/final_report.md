It emphasizes that those who have contributed the least to climate change, often marginalized communities, are disproportionately affected by its consequences. This principle advocates for equitable distribution of both the burdens and benefits of climate action, ensuring that vulnerable populations are prioritized in decision-making processes related to climate policies [1][4].\n\nThe core principles of climate justice include respect for human rights, equitable sharing of resources, and the need for participatory governance. These principles aim to safeguard the rights of the most affected communities while promoting sustainable development [2][3]. Climate justice movements also highlight the importance of a \"just transition,\" which seeks to shift economies from fossil fuels to renewable energy in a way that supports workers and communities historically reliant on polluting industries [4][5].\n\nBy addressing the root causes of climate change and its social implications, climate justice movements strive to create a fairer world for all, linking environmental sustainability with social equity [1][3].\n\n\n\n![Colorful hands in various skin tones surround a globe, symbolizing the principles of climate justice.](https://feps-europe.eu/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Climate-Justice-principles-2-700x394.png)\n*Colorful hands in various skin tones surround a globe, symbolizing the principles of climate justice.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Climate justice and social justice: Two sides of the same coin - Greenpeace: https://www.greenpeace.org/international/story/58334/climate-justice-and-social-justice-two-sides-of-the-same-coin/  \n[2] Principles of Climate Justice - Mary Robinson Foundation: https://www.mrfcj.org/principles-of-climate-justice/  \n[3] What is Climate Justice? - UC Center for Climate Justice: https://centerclimatejustice.universityofcalifornia.edu/what-is-climate-justice/  \n[4] Climate Justice - MIT Climate Portal: https://climate.mit.edu/explainers/climate-justice  \n[5] What is climate justice? - IQAir Foundation: https://www.iqairfoundation.org/resources-climate\n\n## Historical Context of Climate Justice Movements\n\nThe climate justice movement has evolved significantly since its inception, shaped by key events and milestones. Its roots can be traced back to the environmental movements of the 1960s and 1970s, which highlighted the disproportionate impacts of environmental degradation on marginalized communities. The publication of Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* in 1962 galvanized public awareness about environmental issues, leading to the establishment of organizations like Greenpeace and the first Earth Day in 1970 [3][5].\n\nIn the 1990s, the formation of the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) marked a pivotal moment in international climate negotiations. Systemic inequality manifests in the exclusion of marginalized groups from environmental decision-making processes, leading to policies that fail to protect their interests and rights [1]. This exclusion is often rooted in historical injustices, where colonial legacies continue to shape the socio-economic landscape, exacerbating vulnerabilities in the Global South [2][5].\n\nColonialism has not only contributed to environmental degradation but has also entrenched racial and gender hierarchies that further marginalize specific communities. For instance, Indigenous and Afro-descendant populations, who have historically contributed the least to climate change, are often the most affected by its consequences [3][4]. The climate crisis disproportionately impacts these communities, highlighting the urgent need for reparative justice and equitable climate action that acknowledges historical responsibilities [2][5]. \n\nUltimately, climate justice movements advocate for a holistic approach that integrates social, racial, and environmental justice, ensuring that all voices are heard and that solutions are equitable and inclusive [1][3].\n\n\n\n![Youth and community members protesting for climate justice, highlighting systemic inequality and the impacts of colonialism.](https://www.nonviolent-conflict.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/Uganda-Climate-Change-protest--300x225.jpeg)\n*Youth and community members protesting for climate justice, highlighting systemic inequality and the impacts of colonialism.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Environmental Racism and Climate (In)Justice in the Anthropocene: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10551-024-05723-x  \n[2] Historical responsibilities of colonialism: Gender justice as a path to climate justice: https://www.rifs-potsdam.de/en/blog/2022/04/colonialism-gender-climate-justice  \n[3] Climate Justice in Racial Formations - Number Analytics: https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/climate-justice-in-racial-formations  \n[4] Confronting the 'two-headed monster' of environmental injustice: https://sustainability.stanford.edu/news/confronting-two-headed-monster-environmental-injustice  \n[5] Racism and Climate (In)Justice: https://us.boell.org/sites/default/files/2021-03/FINAL%20-%20Racism%20and%20Climate%20%28In%29Justice%20Framing%20Paper.pdf\n\n## Case Studies of Climate Justice Movements\n\nClimate justice movements aim to address the disproportionate impacts of climate change on marginalized communities.
