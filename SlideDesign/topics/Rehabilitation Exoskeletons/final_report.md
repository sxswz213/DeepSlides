These devices are engineered to support and enhance the user's movement, allowing them to regain mobility and independence. The design of rehabilitation exoskeletons typically includes a combination of rigid structures and compliant mechanisms, which help in adapting to the user's movements while providing necessary support.\n\nThe functionality of these exoskeletons is based on advanced technologies such as sensors, actuators, and control systems. Sensors detect the user's intentions, while actuators provide the necessary force to assist movement. Recent advancements have focused on improving the interaction between the user and the exoskeleton, ensuring safety and comfort during use. Various models exist, each with unique features tailored to different rehabilitation needs, such as the ReWalk™ and Ekso™ systems, which have shown effectiveness in clinical settings for enhancing walking capabilities [1][2][3].\n\nThe ongoing research aims to refine these technologies, making them more efficient and accessible for broader applications in rehabilitation [4][5].\n\n\n\n![A robotic exoskeleton with motorized joints designed to assist with rehabilitation and mobility support.](https://sms.hest.ethz.ch/research/past-research-projects/lower-limb-exoskeletons-and-exosuits/modular-lower-limb-exoskeletons/_jcr_content/par/fullwidthimage/image.imageformat.fullwidth.615838589.jpg)\n*A robotic exoskeleton with motorized joints designed to assist with rehabilitation and mobility support.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] The role of lower limb exoskeletons in rehabilitation: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10833196.2025.2465937?src=  \n[2] Lower-Limb Medical and Rehabilitation Exoskeletons: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33961563/  \n[3] Exoskeletons' design and usefulness evidence according to a systematic review: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26340538/  \n[4] Improving Exoskeleton Functionality: Design and Implementation: https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9717/11/12/3278  \n[5] Compliant lower limb exoskeletons: a comprehensive review: https://jneuroengrehab.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12984-019-0517-9\n\n## Applications in Neurorehabilitation\n\nRehabilitation exoskeletons are increasingly utilized in clinical settings to assist patients with lower-extremity impairments, particularly those recovering from strokes or spinal cord injuries. Studies have shown that lower-limb exoskeletons can significantly improve walking speed, balance, and overall functional mobility in patients with neurological conditions [1][2].\n\nFor instance, a systematic review highlighted that exoskeletons employing assistive control strategies yielded the most effective outcomes for stroke patients, particularly when combined with traditional rehabilitation methods [3]. In clinical trials, patients using exoskeletons demonstrated improved gait patterns and increased independence in mobility tasks [4]. Studies indicate that these devices significantly enhance walking speed, balance, and endurance. For instance, a systematic review found that robot-assisted training improved lower limb motor function, walking ability, and balance in stroke patients, with statistically significant results across various metrics, including the Fugl-Meyer Assessment and the Berg Balance Scale [2][3].\n\nMoreover, exoskeletons provide a unique advantage by allowing for high-intensity, repetitive training, which is crucial for neuroplasticity and recovery. While some studies report mixed results regarding the overall effectiveness compared to traditional therapies, the consensus is that exoskeletons can safely augment rehabilitation efforts, particularly for patients with significant mobility impairments [5].\n\nIn summary, rehabilitation exoskeletons represent a valuable tool in enhancing recovery outcomes, offering patients a more engaging and effective rehabilitation experience.\n\n\n\n![A person with a spinal cord injury is supported by robotic exoskeletons to improve walking speed, balance, and endurance for better rehabilitation outcomes.](https://www.medica-tradefair.com/medicacache/pica/1/2/2/2/8/7/1/40021101710233887/exoskeleton-rehabilitation-1200x600.jpg)\n*A person with a spinal cord injury is supported by robotic exoskeletons to improve walking speed, balance, and endurance for better rehabilitation outcomes.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Comparative efficacy of robotic exoskeleton and conventional gait ...: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12121209/  \n[2] Effect of robot-assisted training for lower limb rehabilitation on lower ...: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/human-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnhum.2025.1549379/full  \n[3] The Effectiveness of Overground Robot Exoskeleton Gait Training ...: https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3425/14/8/834  \n[4] Efficacy of an exoskeleton-based physical therapy program for non ...: https://jneuroengrehab.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12984-021-00942-z  \n[5] Overground robotic exoskeleton vs conventional therapy in inpatient ...: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11702026/\n\n## Challenges and Future Directions\n\nThe implementation of rehabilitation exoskeletons faces several challenges that hinder their widespread adoption.
