Four common types include cyclones, scrubbers, electrostatic precipitators, and baghouse filters.\n\nCyclones operate by creating a vortex that separates larger particulates from the airstream. As the dirty air enters tangentially, centrifugal force pushes heavier particles against the chamber walls, allowing them to drop into a collection hopper. While effective for coarse particles, cyclones are often used as pre-cleaners due to their limited efficiency for smaller particulates [1][2].\n\nScrubbers, particularly wet scrubbers, utilize liquid sprays to capture and neutralize pollutants. The gas stream passes through a mist of liquid, which absorbs harmful substances. This method is particularly effective for acidic gases and can achieve high removal efficiencies [1][4].\n\nElectrostatic precipitators (ESPs) use high-voltage electric fields to charge particles, which are then attracted to oppositely charged plates. This technology is highly efficient, often exceeding 99% removal rates for fine particulates, making it popular in power plants [1][5].\n\nBaghouse filters consist of fabric bags that trap particulates as air passes through. They can achieve nearly 100% efficiency for particles as small as 1 micrometer, but require regular maintenance to ensure optimal performance [1][3].\n\n\n\n![A detailed illustration of a fabric-filter baghouse system, highlighting its components for effective air pollution control.](https://cdn.britannica.com/99/27099-004-1D079347/Baghouse-array-fabric-bags-airstream.jpg)\n*A detailed illustration of a fabric-filter baghouse system, highlighting its components for effective air pollution control.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Air pollution control | Solutions, Effects & Prevention - Britannica: https://www.britannica.com/technology/air-pollution-control  \n[2] AIR POLLUTION CONTROL – PARTICULATE MATTER: https://airknowledge.gov/ILT/PERM242/Current/CI/03PERM242_Handout_PM_Control.pdf  \n[3] Air Pollution Control Equipment - EPA Archives: https://archive.epa.gov/region6/6pd/rcra_c/pd-o/web/pdf/a4a-apc-equipment.pdf  \n[4] Air Pollution Control Equipment: Types and Applications: https://www.iqsdirectory.com/articles/air-pollution-control.html  \n[5] Understanding Air Pollution Control Equipment - Thomasnet: https://www.thomasnet.com/articles/plant-facility-equipment/understanding-air-pollution-control-equipment/\n\n## Gaseous Pollutant Control Methods\n\nGaseous pollutants are primarily controlled through three main technologies: absorption, adsorption, and incineration. Each method operates on distinct principles and has varying effectiveness.\n\n**Absorption** involves transferring gaseous pollutants into a liquid solvent. This process is commonly executed using wet scrubbers, where the gas flows through a liquid that captures the pollutants. For instance, flue gas desulfurization (FGD) systems utilize this method to remove sulfur dioxide from emissions, achieving efficiencies of 90-95% in many cases [1][4].\n\n**Adsorption**, on the other hand, is a surface phenomenon where gas molecules adhere to the surface of a solid material, typically activated carbon. This method is particularly effective for controlling volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and can achieve removal efficiencies exceeding 95% [1][4]. \n\n**Incineration** is a combustion process that converts gaseous pollutants into carbon dioxide and water. This method is highly effective, with incineration systems designed to ensure complete combustion, thus minimizing harmful emissions [1][5]. \n\nThese technologies can be used individually or in combination, depending on the specific pollutants and regulatory requirements.\n\n\n\n![Methods for controlling gaseous pollutants from stationary sources.](https://image.slidesharecdn.com/controlofairpollutants-181124103155/85/Control-of-air-pollutants-2-320.jpg)\n*Methods for controlling gaseous pollutants from stationary sources.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Air pollution control - Gases, Emissions, Solutions | Britannica: https://www.britannica.com/technology/air-pollution-control/Control-of-gases  \n[2] Control of Gaseous Emissions - epa nepis: https://nepis.epa.gov/Exe/ZyPURL.cgi?Dockey=9101X626.TXT  \n[3] [PDF] Control of Gaseous Emissions Student Manual: https://www.4cleanair.org/wp-content/uploads/APTI/415combined.pdf  \n[4] Air Pollution Control Technology Review: Absorption: https://www.cpilink.com/blog/air-pollution-control-technology-review-absorption  \n[5] Incineration Processes and Environmental Releases - NCBI: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK233627/\n\n## Comparative Analysis of Technologies\n\nAir pollution control technologies vary significantly in their effectiveness, cost, and applicability. Among the most notable methods are thermal combustion, catalytic oxidation, and photocatalytic oxidation (PCO). Thermal combustion is highly effective for a wide range of pollutants but can be costly, especially for low-volume emissions. Catalytic oxidation offers a balance between efficiency and cost, particularly for volatile organic compounds (VOCs) but may require additional equipment for certain pollutants.\n\nPhotocatalytic oxidation, on the other hand, is particularly suited for low concentration waste streams (up to 1000 ppm) and has lower operating costs compared to other technologies. It is modular, making it flexible for various applications, although it does not benefit from economies of scale like larger systems do. Studies indicate that while PCO systems have higher capital costs, their operational costs are significantly lower, making them attractive for low-flow-rate applications [1][2][4]. \n\nOverall, the choice of technology should be guided by specific emission characteristics, regulatory requirements, and economic considerations, ensuring that the selected method aligns with both environmental goals and budget constraints.\n\n\n\n![Comparison of twelve advanced air pollution control technologies, showcasing innovations and their respective effectiveness and costs.](https://sensor1stop.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/Effective-Air-Pollution-Control.jpeg)\n*Comparison of twelve advanced air pollution control technologies, showcasing innovations and their respective effectiveness and costs.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Cost Comparison with Other Air Pollution Control Technologies: https://docs.nrel.gov/docs/legosti/old/7014.pdf  \n[2] Evaluating the Cost-Effectiveness of Air Pollution Mitigation Strategies: https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/22/6/926  \n[3] Determining When a Best Available Technology Cost - Ohio.gov: https://dam.assets.ohio.gov/image/upload/epa.ohio.gov/Portals/27/engineer/eguides/EG-89.pdf  \n[4] The costs, health and economic impact of air pollution control strategies: https://ghrp.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s41256-024-00373-y  \n[5] Effective options for addressing air quality–related environmental issues: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9463589/\n\n# Air Pollution Control Technologies\n\nAir pollution poses significant risks to public health and the environment, necessitating effective control technologies.
