This transition is characterized by a fundamental redesign of food systems, emphasizing the integration of ecological principles into agricultural practices. The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has identified ten elements that guide this transition, including the promotion of biodiversity, soil health, and sustainable resource management [1].\n\nKey principles of agroecology include recycling nutrients, diversifying crops, and minimizing external inputs. These principles are not merely theoretical; they are actionable strategies that farmers can implement to enhance resilience against climate change and improve food security [2]. The transition is often framed within three operational principles: ecological sustainability, social equity, and economic viability, which collectively aim to create a just and sustainable food system [3].\n\nFrameworks for understanding agro-ecological transitions often highlight the interconnectedness of various components, including ecological practices, socio-economic factors, and governance structures. This method not only enhances scientific understanding but also empowers communities by integrating their local knowledge and experiences into the research process, leading to more relevant and sustainable solutions [1][4].\n\nThe co-creation of knowledge is essential in agroecology, as it allows stakeholders to engage actively in decision-making processes.
