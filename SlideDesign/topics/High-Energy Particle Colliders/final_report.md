The journey began in the early 20th century with the development of the first particle accelerators, such as the cyclotron invented by Ernest Lawrence in 1930, which accelerated particles using magnetic fields. This was followed by the invention of the betatron by Donald Kerst in 1940, which allowed for the acceleration of electrons to high energies.\n\nIn the 1960s, the first electron-positron collider, AdA, was built in Italy by Bruno Touschek, paving the way for future colliders. The Intersecting Storage Ring (ISR) at CERN, operational from 1971, was a groundbreaking development that allowed for proton collisions, leading to the discovery of new particles. The 1974 discovery of the J/ψ particle at both SLAC and Brookhaven National Laboratory was a pivotal moment, confirming the existence of the charm quark.\n\nThe Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP) and the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) further advanced the field, enabling unprecedented energy levels and discoveries, including the Higgs boson in 2012. These advancements illustrate the continuous innovation in collider technology and its critical role in understanding the fundamental components of matter.\n\n\n\n![An infographic detailing the evolution of particle colliders, emphasizing the Large Hadron Collider's role in exploring fundamental particles.](https://www.modern-physics.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/large_hadron_collider_-_lhc.png)\n*An infographic detailing the evolution of particle colliders, emphasizing the Large Hadron Collider's role in exploring fundamental particles.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] History of Particle Colliders - Institute of Physics: https://www.iop.org/sites/default/files/2019-04/newsletter-jan-2018-colliders.pdf  \n[2] Short History of Particle Accelerators: https://cas.web.cern.ch/sites/default/files/lectures/zakopane-2006/tazzari-history.pdf  \n[3] Timeline of particle discoveries - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_particle_discoveries  \n[4] Accelerator timeline: http://www.accelerators-for-society.org/about-accelerators/timeliner/timeline.php  \n[5] The history of CERN | timeline.web.cern.ch: https://timeline.web.cern.ch/timeline-header/89\n\n## Types of Particle Colliders\n\nParticle colliders are essential tools in high-energy physics, designed to accelerate and collide particles at high speeds. There are two primary types of colliders: linear and circular. \n\nLinear accelerators, or LINACs, propel particles along a straight path. They are particularly effective for applications requiring precise control of particle beams, such as in fixed-target experiments and medical therapies, including cancer treatment using X-rays and protons [1][4]. \n\nIn contrast, circular accelerators, such as cyclotrons and synchrotrons, propel particles around a circular track. This design allows particles to gain energy with each revolution, making them suitable for high-energy collisions. Circular colliders can achieve higher collision energies, which is crucial for exploring fundamental particles and forces in the universe [2][3]. The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN is the most notable example, capable of colliding protons at unprecedented energies [5].\n\nBoth types of colliders play vital roles in advancing our understanding of particle physics and have numerous applications in medicine and industry [1][4].\n\n\n\n![Illustration of various particle accelerators, highlighting their components.](https://i.ytimg.com/vi/vKcTMwJSs4s/hq720.jpg?sqp=-oaymwEhCK4FEIIDSFryq4qpAxMIARUAAAAAGAElAADIQj0AgKJD&rs=AOn4CLADw2qqRNliqIpqqAMd4RHCTMqVAg)\n*Illustration of various particle accelerators, highlighting their components.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] How Particle Accelerators Work | Department of Energy: https://www.energy.gov/articles/how-particle-accelerators-work  \n[2] Types of particle accelerators | Particle Physics Class Notes - Fiveable: https://library.fiveable.me/particle-physics/unit-9/types-particle-accelerators/study-guide/gHpzNDonXrphy17L  \n[3] Accelerators | CERN: https://home.web.cern.ch/science/accelerators  \n[4] About Particle Accelerators - Brookhaven National Laboratory: https://www.bnl.gov/accelerators/accelerators.php  \n[5] Accelerators are everywhere, perhaps closer than you think…: https://www.scienceinschool.org/article/2024/accelerators-are-everywhere/\n\n## The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)\n\nThe Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is the world's largest and most powerful particle accelerator, located at CERN near Geneva, Switzerland. Spanning approximately 27 kilometers in circumference, it consists of a ring of superconducting magnets and various accelerating structures that propel protons and heavy ions to near-light speeds. The LHC began operations on September 10, 2008, and has since been pivotal in advancing our understanding of fundamental physics, particularly through the discovery of the Higgs boson in 2012, a significant milestone in particle physics.\n\nThe LHC operates by colliding two high-energy particle beams traveling in opposite directions. These collisions occur at four main points around the accelerator, where large detectors like ATLAS and CMS analyze the resulting particle interactions. The LHC's design incorporates advanced technologies, including superconducting electromagnets that require cooling to -271.3°C, making it one of the most complex scientific instruments ever built. Its ongoing research aims to explore unanswered questions in physics, including the nature of dark matter and the fundamental forces of the universe.\n\n\n\n![A detailed diagram of the Large Hadron Collider, showcasing its key components and systems.](https://news2.rice.edu/files/2020/04/0401_LHC-3-WEB.jpg)\n*A detailed diagram of the Large Hadron Collider, showcasing its key components and systems.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Large Hadron Collider - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider  \n[2] The Large Hadron Collider Project: https://cds.cern.ch/record/313675/files/lhc-project-report-53.pdf  \n[3] Review The large hadron collider: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0146641012000695  \n[4] The Large Hadron Collider | CERN: https://home.cern/science/accelerators/large-hadron-collider  \n[5] Facts and figures about the LHC: https://home.cern/resources/faqs/facts-and-figures-about-lhc\n\n## Future of Particle Colliders\n\nThe Future Circular Collider (FCC) is poised to be the next significant advancement in particle physics, following the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). This ambitious project aims to explore energy levels far beyond those currently achievable, with the potential to reach collision energies of up to 100 TeV. The FCC will consist of two main phases: the FCC-ee, an electron-positron collider for precision measurements, and the FCC-hh, a proton-proton collider designed for high-energy collisions. The FCC-ee is expected to operate in the late 2040s for approximately 15 years, followed by the FCC-hh, which will run for about 25 years starting in the 2070s [1][3][5].\n\nIn parallel, China is developing the Circular Electron-Positron Collider (CEPC), which aims to serve as a Higgs factory, producing precise measurements of the Higgs boson and other particles. The CEPC is designed to operate at various energy levels, enhancing our understanding of fundamental physics [2][4]. Both the FCC and CEPC represent critical steps toward unraveling the mysteries of the universe, including dark matter and the fundamental forces governing particle interactions.\n\n\n\n![Cross-sectional illustration of the Future Circular Collider design, showcasing its massive tunnel structure and advanced particle acceleration components.](https://cerncourier.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/CCJulAug23_FCChh_feature.jpg)\n*Cross-sectional illustration of the Future Circular Collider design, showcasing its massive tunnel structure and advanced particle acceleration components.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Future Circular Collider - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_Circular_Collider  \n[2] China's designs for a future circular collider - CERN Courier: https://cerncourier.com/a/chinas-designs-for-a-future-circular-collider/  \n[3] The Future Circular Collider - CERN: https://home.cern/science/accelerators/future-circular-collider  \n[4] FCC Week 2023 - First Announcement | Future Circular Collider: https://fcc.web.cern.ch/fcc-week-2023-first-announcement  \n[5] Future Circular Collider Documents - CERN Document Server: https://cds.cern.ch/collection/Future%20Circular%20Collider%20Documents?ln=en\n\n# High-Energy Particle Colliders\n\nParticle colliders are pivotal in the field of high-energy physics, enabling scientists to explore the fundamental components of matter and the forces that govern the universe.
