This technology is crucial for powering small devices, particularly in the context of the Internet of Things (IoT). The primary mechanisms of energy harvesting include piezoelectric, triboelectric, and thermoelectric methods.\n\n**Piezoelectric energy harvesting** utilizes materials that generate an electric charge in response to mechanical stress. This method is particularly effective in applications involving vibrations or movements, such as in wearable devices and structural health monitoring systems. Recent advancements in nanomaterials have enhanced the efficiency and applicability of piezoelectric generators, making them suitable for a wide range of environments and conditions [2][3].\n\n**Triboelectric energy harvesting** relies on the contact and separation of different materials to generate electricity through friction. This method is gaining traction for its simplicity and effectiveness in converting mechanical energy from human activities into electrical power [4].\n\n**Thermoelectric energy harvesting** converts temperature differences into electrical energy using the Seebeck effect. Thermoelectric generators (TEGs) are particularly useful in environments with significant temperature gradients, such as industrial settings or automotive applications [1][3]. \n\nThese energy harvesting techniques are increasingly relevant in the development of sustainable, self-powered devices, particularly as the demand for low-power electronics continues to rise.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of a thermoelectric heat engine setup demonstrating energy harvesting through temperature gradients.](https://cdnintech.com/media/chapter/65239/1512345123/media/F4.png)\n*Illustration of a thermoelectric heat engine setup demonstrating energy harvesting through temperature gradients.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] What is Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting? - ONiO: https://www.onio.com/article/what-is-thermoelectric-harvesting.html  \n[2] Introductory Chapter: Piezoelectric Energy Harvesting Based on Two-Dimensional Materials: https://www.intechopen.com/chapters/84335  \n[3] Energy Harvesting in Nanomaterials - Number Analytics: https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/energy-harvesting-nanomaterials-guide  \n[4] Triboelectric and Piezoelectric Nanogenerators for Self-Powered Applications: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9781874/\n\n## Nanostructured Materials in Energy Harvesting\n\nNanostructured materials, particularly graphene and carbon nanotubes (CNTs), play a crucial role in enhancing the efficiency of energy harvesting systems.
