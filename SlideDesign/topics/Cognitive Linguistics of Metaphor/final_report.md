For instance, the metaphor \"life is a journey\" allows individuals to frame life events in terms of travel, suggesting notions of direction, progress, and obstacles. This mapping reveals deep cognitive structures that influence our perceptions and actions.\n\nCMT has significantly impacted various fields, including psychology and linguistics, by clarifying the relationship between language and thought. Critics, however, argue that metaphor comprehension can vary significantly across cultures, challenging the idea of universal applicability. Despite these critiques, CMT remains a pivotal framework for understanding how metaphorical thinking shapes our conceptual landscape, emphasizing that metaphors are not merely linguistic tools but essential cognitive phenomena. \n\nThe theory also highlights the role of embodied experiences in shaping our understanding, as metaphors often draw from our physical interactions with the world, making abstract concepts more relatable and comprehensible.\n\n\n\n![An overview of the Cognitive Metaphor Theory by Lakoff and Johnson, highlighting the pervasive influence of metaphors in our understanding.](https://slideplayer.com/slide/12684825/76/images/2/THE+COGNITIVE+METAPHOR+THEORY:+LAKOFF+AND+JOHNSON+(1980/2003).jpg)\n*An overview of the Cognitive Metaphor Theory by Lakoff and Johnson, highlighting the pervasive influence of metaphors in our understanding.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Lakoff, G., & Johnson, M. For instance, the metaphor \"LIFE IS A JOURNEY\" illustrates how our understanding of life is influenced by travel experiences, mapping physical actions onto abstract notions of existence and progress.\n\nNeuroscientific research supports this view, indicating that the brain processes metaphorical language similarly to literal language, activating sensory and motor areas. Studies show that the right hemisphere is particularly engaged in processing novel metaphors, suggesting a unique neural network for metaphor comprehension. Cognitive linguistics, as proposed by Lakoff and Johnson, posits that metaphors are fundamental to our conceptual system, shaping how we understand abstract ideas through concrete experiences. This perspective emphasizes that metaphors are not mere linguistic embellishments but integral to human thought and communication [1][3].\n\nIn contrast, relevance theory, developed by Sperber and Wilson, focuses on how meaning is derived from metaphorical language based on contextual relevance. It suggests that metaphors often involve \"loose talk,\" where the intended meaning is inferred rather than explicitly stated. This approach highlights the cognitive effort required to interpret metaphors, emphasizing the importance of contextual clues in grasping the speaker's intended implications [2][5].\n\nTogether, these theories enhance our understanding of metaphor. Cognitive linguistics elucidates the structural aspects of metaphorical thought, while relevance theory provides a pragmatic lens for interpreting meaning in communication. This interplay between cognitive structures and contextual interpretation fosters a more comprehensive understanding of metaphor [4][3].\n\n\n\n![A silhouette of a man in a suit with a puzzle-piece shaped opening at the top of his head, symbolizing the integration of cognitive linguistics and relevance theory in metaphor comprehension.](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/51uzIIjs9vL._SL1360_.jpg)\n*A silhouette of a man in a suit with a puzzle-piece shaped opening at the top of his head, symbolizing the integration of cognitive linguistics and relevance theory in metaphor comprehension.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Tendahl, M., & Gibbs, R.
