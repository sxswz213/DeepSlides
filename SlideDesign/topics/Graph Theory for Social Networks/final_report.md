A graph consists of vertices (or nodes) and edges (or links) that connect pairs of vertices. The basic components of a graph are defined as follows:\n\n1. **Vertices (Nodes)**: These are the fundamental units of a graph, representing entities such as people in a social network or cities in a transportation system.\n\n2. **Edges**: These are the connections between vertices. Edges can be classified into two types:\n   - **Directed Edges**: These have a direction, indicating a one-way relationship (e.g., A follows B).\n   - **Undirected Edges**: These do not have a direction, representing a mutual relationship (e.g., A and B are friends).\n\n3. **Weighted Graphs**: In some graphs, edges carry weights, which can represent costs, distances, or capacities. This is useful in applications like route optimization.\n\nGraphs can be finite or infinite, depending on the number of vertices and edges. The most commonly used measures include degree, betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector centrality.\n\n**Degree centrality** quantifies the number of direct connections a node has. Nodes with high degree centrality are often seen as influential due to their ability to connect with many others directly, making them key players in information dissemination [3][5].\n\n**Betweenness centrality** assesses a node's role as a bridge between other nodes. It calculates how often a node appears on the shortest paths between pairs of nodes, highlighting its importance in facilitating communication and resource flow within the network [4][5].\n\n**Closeness centrality** measures how quickly a node can access all other nodes in the network. It is calculated based on the average length of the shortest paths from a node to all others, indicating its potential to spread information rapidly [3][5].\n\n**Eigenvector centrality** goes beyond simple connectivity by considering the influence of a node's connections. A node is deemed influential not just by the number of its connections but also by the quality and influence of those it is connected to [3][4].\n\nThese measures provide valuable insights into the structure and dynamics of social networks, guiding strategic decisions in various applications.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of social network graphs demonstrating varying centrality measures, including degree and eigenvector centrality.](https://cdncontribute.geeksforgeeks.org/wp-content/uploads/EigenvectorCentrality.png)\n*Illustration of social network graphs demonstrating varying centrality measures, including degree and eigenvector centrality.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] How Correlated Are Network Centrality Measures? - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2875682/  \n[2] Centrality - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrality  \n[3] Social Network Analysis: Understanding Centrality Measures: https://cambridge-intelligence.com/keylines-faqs-social-network-analysis/  \n[4] Betweenness Centrality and Other Essential Centrality Measures: https://memgraph.com/blog/betweenness-centrality-and-other-centrality-measures-network-analysis  \n[5] Network Centrality: Understanding Degree, Closeness & Betweenness Centrality: https://visiblenetworklabs.com/2021/04/16/understanding-network-centrality/\n\n## Community Detection Algorithms\n\nCommunity detection algorithms are essential for identifying groups within social networks where nodes are more densely connected to each other than to the rest of the network. Two prominent methods are the Girvan-Newman algorithm and modularity optimization.\n\nThe **Girvan-Newman algorithm** focuses on edge betweenness centrality, which measures the number of shortest paths that pass through an edge. The algorithm iteratively removes edges with the highest betweenness until the network is divided into distinct communities. This method effectively isolates clusters of nodes that are more interconnected, providing insights into the network's structure [1][3].\n\n**Modularity optimization**, on the other hand, quantifies the strength of a community partition. It aims to maximize the modularity score, which reflects the difference between the actual density of links within communities and the expected density if links were distributed randomly. Higher modularity scores indicate better-defined communities [2][4].\n\nBoth methods have their strengths and weaknesses. The Girvan-Newman algorithm is computationally intensive, making it suitable for smaller networks, while modularity optimization is more efficient for larger datasets [3][5].\n\n\n\n![Illustration of community detection through modularity optimization, highlighting distinct clusters within a network.](https://networkx.org/documentation/stable/_images/sphx_glr_plot_girvan_newman_001.png)\n*Illustration of community detection through modularity optimization, highlighting distinct clusters within a network.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Community Detection Algorithms - Jackson State University: https://www.jsums.edu/nmeghanathan/files/2016/01/CSC641-Sp2016-Module-4-Community1.pdf  \n[2] Modularity and community structure in networks - PNAS: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0601602103  \n[3] Top Community Detection Algorithms Compared - Hypermode: https://hypermode.com/blog/community-detection-algorithms  \n[4] Community detection algorithm for social network based on node ... - ScienceDirect: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0952197624001052  \n[5] (PDF) Community detection in social networks - ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/295395520_Community_detection_in_social_networks\n\n## Applications of Graph Theory in Social Media\n\nGraph theory plays a crucial role in enhancing the functionality of social media platforms.
