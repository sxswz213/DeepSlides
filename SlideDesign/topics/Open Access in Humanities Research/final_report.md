The three primary models of open access are Gold, Green, and Diamond. \n\n**Gold Open Access** involves publishing articles in OA journals, where costs are typically covered by Article Processing Charges (APCs) paid by authors or their institutions. This model ensures immediate access to published works but can create financial barriers for researchers without institutional support [1][3]. \n\n**Green Open Access**, or self-archiving, allows authors to deposit their manuscripts in institutional or disciplinary repositories. This model can include preprints or postprints and may be subject to embargo periods set by publishers [2][4]. \n\n**Diamond Open Access** is a newer model that eliminates fees for both authors and readers, often supported by community-driven initiatives. This model promotes equitable access to knowledge and is gaining traction, particularly in regions like Latin America [5][4]. \n\nHistorically, the open access movement began in the late 1980s and early 1990s, with significant milestones such as the Budapest Open Access Initiative in 2001 and the Berlin Declaration in 2003, advocating for unrestricted access to scholarly literature [4][5]. The evolution of these models reflects a growing commitment to making research accessible to all, fostering a more inclusive academic environment.\n\n\n\n![The benefits of open access in humanities research, emphasizing increased visibility and compliance with funder policies.](https://www.yearofopen.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/open-access-benefits.png)\n*The benefits of open access in humanities research, emphasizing increased visibility and compliance with funder policies.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Open Access colours: green, gold, diamond, hybrid and more - https://onderzoektips.ugent.be/en/tips/00000461/  \n[2] Green, Gold, and Diamond Open Access - https://open-access.network/en/information/open-access-primers/green-and-gold  \n[3] Green, Gold, Diamond? As of December 2023, only about 18% of humanities and social sciences journals are classified as OA, compared to nearly 70% in physical and mathematical sciences [1][2]. This gap arises partly because humanities research often favors longer formats, such as monographs, which are less compatible with traditional OA models [2][5].\n\nHumanities researchers encounter unique challenges, including limited awareness of OA options and the complexities of self-archiving. Many scholars are uncertain about the policies governing their ability to share their work, leading to underutilization of available OA platforms [3][4]. The fragmented nature of humanities publishing, with diverse formats and languages, further complicates the visibility of OA outputs [3][5]. Despite these hurdles, the potential benefits of OA—such as increased visibility and accessibility of research—are crucial for fostering a more inclusive academic environment [1][4].\n\nEmerging OA models, such as diamond and platinum OA, offer promising alternatives that could enhance accessibility for humanities scholars [2].\n\n\n\n![Pathways for open access publishing, outlining the roles of researchers and the routes for publishing in open access or subscription-based journals.](https://libapps-au.s3-ap-southeast-2.amazonaws.com/accounts/113543/images/Open-Access-Publishing-1024x805.gif)\n*Pathways for open access publishing, outlining the roles of researchers and the routes for publishing in open access or subscription-based journals.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] A year in review: open access at OUP - Oxford University Press. https://corp.oup.com/feature/a-year-in-review-open-access-at-oup/  \n[2] Dis-counting Open Access in the Humanities - Issuu. https://issuu.com/against-the-grain/docs/feb_2024_v36-1/s/45026912  \n[3] Open access - Wikipedia. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_access  \n[4] Open access publishing for humanities researchers | Editage Insights. https://www.editage.com/insights/open-access-publishing-for-humanities-researchers  \n[5] Humanities scholars can act now to go open access (opinion). https://www.insidehighered.com/views/2022/04/13/humanities-scholars-can-act-now-go-open-access-opinion\n\n## Benefits and Challenges of Open Access\n\nOpen Access (OA) publishing offers significant advantages for humanities researchers, primarily through increased visibility and citation rates.
