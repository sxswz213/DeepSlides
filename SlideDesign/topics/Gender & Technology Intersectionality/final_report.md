Globally, women are 8% less likely than men to own a mobile phone, and over 1.1 billion women lack mobile internet access, which exacerbates existing inequalities in education, employment, and social participation [1][2]. This gap is not merely a matter of access; it also encompasses the quality of digital experiences, where women often encounter online harassment and violence, leading to self-censorship and reduced participation in digital spaces [1][4].\n\nSocial norms and structural inequalities further hinder women's digital inclusion. In many cultures, traditional roles restrict women's access to technology and education, perpetuating a cycle of disadvantage [3][4]. Addressing these issues requires a multi-faceted approach, including improving digital infrastructure, promoting digital literacy, and creating supportive environments that encourage women's participation in technology [1][2]. Studies indicate that between 16% and 58% of women and girls have experienced some form of technology-facilitated violence, including cyberbullying, doxing, and image-based abuse [1][2][5]. The anonymity and vast reach of digital platforms enable perpetrators to inflict harm without immediate consequences, often leading to severe psychological and physical repercussions for victims [3][4].\n\nRangita de Silva de Alwis emphasizes that technologies like artificial intelligence can be weaponized to create deepfakes, further objectifying women and facilitating abuse [1]. This digital violence not only impacts individual victims but also discourages women's participation in public life, as many fear the repercussions of online harassment [2][3]. To combat this pervasive issue, it is essential to integrate a gendered perspective into legal frameworks and promote safer digital environments [1][4]. \n\nEfforts must focus on raising awareness, enhancing reporting mechanisms, and fostering collaboration between governments, tech companies, and civil society to create a safer online space for all [2][5].\n\n\n\n![A graphic emphasizing that technology can facilitate gender-based violence and threaten women's and girls' rights and wellbeing.](https://unric.org/en/wp-content/uploads/sites/15/2023/11/English_-_2023_UNWomen_16-Days-of-Activisim_IG-Carousel_frame1.png)\n*A graphic emphasizing that technology can facilitate gender-based violence and threaten women's and girls' rights and wellbeing.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Gender Violence in the Digital Age | The Regulatory Review: https://www.theregreview.org/2025/02/23/spotlight-gender-violence-in-the-digital-age/  \n[2] Creating safe digital spaces free of trolls, doxing, and hate speech: https://www.unwomen.org/en/articles/explainer/creating-safe-digital-spaces-free-of-trolls-doxing-and-hate-speech  \n[3] How Technology-Facilitated Gender-Based Violence Impacts Women and Girls: https://unric.org/en/how-technology-facilitated-gender-based-violence-impacts-women-and-girls/  \n[4] Technology-Facilitated Gender-Based Violence: https://giwps.georgetown.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/Technology-Facilitated-Gender-Based-Violence.pdf  \n[5] FAQs: Digital abuse, trolling, stalking, and other forms of technology-facilitated violence against women: https://www.unwomen.org/en/articles/faqs/digital-abuse-trolling-stalking-and-other-forms-of-technology-facilitated-violence-against-women\n\n## Data Governance and Gender\n\nData governance plays a crucial role in shaping gender equality within technology. Biased data collection practices can reinforce stereotypes and discrimination, particularly against women. For instance, AI systems trained on skewed datasets often perpetuate harmful gender norms, reflecting societal biases. A study by UN Women highlights that only 30% of the AI workforce is female, limiting diverse perspectives in technology development and governance, which exacerbates gender disparities in AI outcomes [5].\n\nWomen in data governance face significant challenges, including underrepresentation in leadership roles and pervasive stereotypes. These barriers hinder their ability to influence policies that could mitigate gender biases in technology [2]. To combat these issues, a multistakeholder approach that prioritizes gender equity in data governance frameworks is essential. This includes promoting transparency, accountability, and ethical decision-making in AI systems to ensure equitable service for all genders [1][3]. Digital economies can empower women by offering flexible work arrangements and access to new job markets. Research indicates that the digital economy positively impacts female employment rates, particularly in developing countries, where focused policy interventions can enhance these benefits by providing training and financial incentives for companies that hire women in digital roles[1][3].\n\nHowever, these advancements also risk exacerbating existing inequalities. Women are often overrepresented in low-paying, precarious jobs that are vulnerable to automation, while high-growth sectors like STEM remain male-dominated due to persistent gender biases[2][4]. Furthermore, women are less likely to receive on-the-job training compared to men, limiting their ability to adapt to new technologies and secure better positions[5]. The rise of gig and platform work can lead to unstable employment conditions, where women's contributions may go unrecognized and undervalued. \n\nTo create a more equitable future of work, it is essential to invest in women's education, training, and access to technology, ensuring they are not left behind in the evolving job landscape.\n\n\n\n![The benefits of the digital economy include job creation and expansion of business opportunities.](https://businessjargons.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/benefits-of-digital-economy.jpg)\n*The benefits of the digital economy include job creation and expansion of business opportunities.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Does the digital economy improve female employment?
