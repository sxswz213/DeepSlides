These diseases can arise from various pathogens, including bacteria, viruses, parasites, and fungi. Common examples include rabies, Ebola, and Lyme disease. The transmission pathways for zoonotic diseases are diverse, including direct contact with infected animals, bites, consumption of contaminated food or water, and vector-borne routes through insects like ticks and mosquitoes.\n\nResearch indicates that nearly two-thirds of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic, with a significant portion originating from wildlife. For instance, vector-borne transmission is particularly prevalent in zoonoses linked to land-use changes, where 52.5% of cases are transmitted this way. Other pathways include direct animal contact (23.8%) and airborne transmission (19.8%) [1][2][3]. Understanding these transmission routes is crucial for developing effective surveillance and control strategies to mitigate the risks associated with zoonotic diseases.\n\n\n\n![Transmission methods of zoonotic diseases, highlighting vector-borne, airborne, and direct contact pathways.](https://blogs.egu.eu/divisions/cl/files/2020/03/Figure2.png)\n*Transmission methods of zoonotic diseases, highlighting vector-borne, airborne, and direct contact pathways.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Targeting Transmission Pathways for Emerging Zoonotic Disease Surveillance and Control - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4507309/  \n[2] Zoonotic disease | Definition, Characteristics, Examples, & Facts - Britannica: https://www.britannica.com/science/zoonotic-disease  \n[3] Zoonotic Diseases: Types, Transmission & Treatment - Cleveland Clinic: https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/zoonotic-diseases  \n[4] Zoonotic diseases: understanding the risks and mitigating the threats - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10546628/  \n[5] Transmission of Zoonoses Between Animals and Humans - Merck Veterinary Manual: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/public-health/zoonoses/transmission-of-zoonoses-between-animals-and-humans\n\n## Factors Contributing to Zoonotic Disease Emergence\n\nZoonotic diseases are increasingly emerging due to a combination of environmental changes, urbanization, and climate change. Human activities such as deforestation, urban expansion, and wildlife exploitation disrupt natural habitats, leading to increased interactions between humans and wildlife. This heightened contact raises the risk of zoonotic disease transmission, as pathogens can jump from animals to humans more easily in altered environments [3][4].\n\nClimate change plays a significant role in this dynamic. Rising temperatures and extreme weather events expand the habitats of disease-carrying vectors like mosquitoes and ticks, facilitating the spread of diseases such as malaria and dengue fever [2][5]. Additionally, urbanization often leads to habitat loss and biodiversity decline, which can create conditions that favor the proliferation of certain pathogens [3][4]. \n\nUnderstanding these interconnected factors is crucial for developing effective surveillance and prevention strategies to mitigate the risks associated with zoonotic diseases [1][3].\n\n\n\n![The cycle of climate change influencing zoonotic diseases through expanded wildlife urban interfaces and increased biodiversity.](https://www.sciencepolicyjournal.org/uploads/5/4/3/4/5434385/fig1-philson-etal-jspg-19-1-climatechange-zoonoticdiseases-expand_orig.png)\n*The cycle of climate change influencing zoonotic diseases through expanded wildlife urban interfaces and increased biodiversity.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Review Effects of environmental change on zoonotic disease risk: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1471492214000324  \n[2] New report highlights the impact of changes in environment on One Health: https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-07-2022-new-report-highlights-the-impact-of-changes-in-environment-on-one-health  \n[3] The Impact of Human Activities on Zoonotic Infection Transmissions: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10215220/  \n[4] Climate Change, Super-Driver of Zoonotic Diseases Emergence: https://prezode-initiative.org/en/climate-change-super-driver-of-zoonotic-diseases-emergence/  \n[5] Climate change is driving the expansion of zoonotic diseases: https://researchoutreach.org/articles/climate-change-driving-expansion-zoonotic-diseases-2/\n\n## Impact of Zoonotic Diseases on Public Health and Economy\n\nZoonotic diseases, which are transmitted from animals to humans, pose significant threats to public health and the economy. Approximately 60% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic, underscoring the urgent need for effective surveillance and prevention strategies [1][3].\n\nThe economic implications are profound. Outbreaks can disrupt trade, strain healthcare systems, and lead to substantial financial losses in agriculture and tourism. Furthermore, zoonotic diseases can affect animal welfare, leading to increased mortality rates in livestock and wildlife, which in turn impacts food security and livelihoods [4].\n\nIn summary, the impact of zoonotic diseases extends beyond health concerns, significantly affecting economies and animal welfare, necessitating a comprehensive approach to surveillance and management.\n\n\n\n![A world map illustrating five zoonotic diseases and their associated animal reservoirs.](https://healthforanimals.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/Five-Zoonotic-Diseases-Across-the-World.png)\n*A world map illustrating five zoonotic diseases and their associated animal reservoirs.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Zoonotic Diseases: Impacts on Animal, Human, and Public Health: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392713065_Zoonotic_Diseases_Impacts_on_Animal_Human_and_Public_Health  \n[2] ZOONOTIC DISEASES, HUMAN HEALTH AND FARM ANIMAL WELFARE: https://www.ciwf.org.uk/media/3756123/Zoonotic-diseases-human-health-and-farm-animal-welfare-16-page-report.pdf  \n[3] The Impact of Human Activities on Zoonotic Infection Transmissions: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10215220/  \n[4] The impacts of animal agriculture on One Health—Bacterial zoonoses: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352771424000740\n\n## Zoonotic Disease Surveillance Strategies\n\nZoonotic diseases, which are transmitted between animals and humans, pose significant public health challenges. This strategy promotes collaboration among various sectors to enhance surveillance and response efforts.\n\nCurrent surveillance methods include integrated systems that monitor disease patterns across species and environments.
