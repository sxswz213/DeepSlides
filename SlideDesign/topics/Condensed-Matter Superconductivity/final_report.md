This unique state allows electrical currents to flow indefinitely without energy loss, a property that has significant implications for technology and energy efficiency. \n\nOne of the defining characteristics of superconductors is the Meissner effect, which occurs when a superconductor expels magnetic fields from its interior upon transitioning to the superconducting state. This effect was first observed by Walther Meißner and Robert Ochsenfeld in 1933, demonstrating that superconductors are not merely perfect conductors but possess unique electromagnetic properties. The Meissner effect is crucial for applications such as magnetic levitation and superconducting magnets, as it allows for the stable exclusion of magnetic fields, maintaining the superconducting state under specific conditions.\n\nIn summary, superconductivity is characterized by zero resistance and the Meissner effect, both of which arise from the collective behavior of electrons in a superconducting material, leading to significant advancements in various fields, including energy transmission and magnetic technologies.\n\n\n\n![The Meissner effect in superconductors, demonstrating the expulsion of magnetic fields when cooled below the critical temperature.](https://sciencenotes.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Meissner-Effect-in-Superconductors.png)\n*The Meissner effect in superconductors, demonstrating the expulsion of magnetic fields when cooled below the critical temperature.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Zero resistance phenomenon - (Principles of Physics II): https://library.fiveable.me/key-terms/principles-physics-ii/zero-resistance-phenomenon  \n[2] Meissner effect: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissner_effect  \n[3] Superconductors are materials that exhibit zero (or close to ...: https://www.physics.usu.edu/peak/phys_2710/mn/mn_9.pdf  \n[4] 9.9: Superconductivity: https://phys.libretexts.org/Bookshelves/University_Physics/University_Physics_(OpenStax)/University_Physics_III_-_Optics_and_Modern_Physics_(OpenStax)/09%3A_Condensed_Matter_Physics/9.09%3A_Superconductivity  \n[5] Organic Superconductors: Bechgaard Salts, Background ...: https://hoffman.physics.harvard.edu/materials/organic/background.php\n\n## Types of Superconductors\n\nSuperconductors are classified into two main types: Type I and Type II, each exhibiting distinct properties and behaviors. \n\nType I superconductors, often referred to as conventional superconductors, are typically pure metals that exhibit superconductivity at very low temperatures, usually below 10 K. They completely expel magnetic fields, a phenomenon known as the Meissner effect, and transition sharply from a superconducting state to a normal state when exposed to a critical magnetic field. Examples include mercury and lead, which are characterized by their simplicity and well-understood physical properties, making them easier to study and model [1][3].\n\nIn contrast, Type II superconductors, also known as high-temperature superconductors, can operate at higher temperatures and allow partial penetration of magnetic fields in a mixed state. This occurs between two critical magnetic fields, enabling them to maintain superconductivity under stronger magnetic conditions. Type II superconductors are often alloys or complex oxides, such as niobium-titanium and yttrium barium copper oxide, and are favored for applications requiring high magnetic fields, like MRI machines and maglev trains [2][4][5].\n\nThe differences in their magnetic field behavior and temperature ranges make Type I and Type II superconductors suitable for different technological applications.\n\n\n\n![Comparison of Type I and Type II superconductors showing their magnetic field behaviors and phases.](https://image4.slideserve.com/8815425/type-i-and-type-ii-superconductors-l.jpg)\n*Comparison of Type I and Type II superconductors showing their magnetic field behaviors and phases.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Comparison of Type – I and Type – II Superconductors: https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/electrical-engineering/comparison-of-type-i-and-type-ii-superconductors/  \n[2] Type I vs Type II: https://www.doitpoms.ac.uk/tlplib/superconductivity/type.php  \n[3] 1.3 Types of Superconductors (Type I and Type II) - Fiveable: https://library.fiveable.me/superconducting-devices/unit-1/types-superconductors-type-type-ii/study-guide/E2Vgau1IuL4mBMwF  \n[4] Comparison of Type - I and Type - II Superconductors - Electrical4U: https://www.electrical4u.com/comparison-of-type-i-and-type-ii-superconductors/  \n[5] Differences between Type I and II superconductors | by Bharat Ambati: https://medium.com/@bharatambati/differences-between-type-i-and-ii-superconductors-93b519cd1e1b\n\n## BCS Theory and Microscopic Mechanisms\n\nThe Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory, formulated in 1957, revolutionized the understanding of superconductivity by explaining how certain materials can conduct electricity without resistance at low temperatures. Central to this theory is the concept of Cooper pairs—pairs of electrons that interact through lattice vibrations, allowing them to move cohesively and reduce energy loss during conduction. This phenomenon occurs when an electron distorts the surrounding lattice, creating a region of higher positive charge density that attracts another electron, leading to pairing despite the inherent Coulomb repulsion between them.\n\nThe BCS theory posits that these Cooper pairs condense into a collective ground state, resulting in a macroscopic quantum effect. This state is characterized by a gap in the energy spectrum, meaning that a finite amount of energy is required to break a pair, thus preventing resistance. The implications of BCS theory extend beyond superconductivity, influencing fields such as nuclear physics and cosmology, and paving the way for practical applications like lossless power transmission and advanced magnetic levitation technologies.\n\n\n\n![Illustration of the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory of superconductivity, highlighting the formation and movement of Cooper pairs.](https://www.modern-physics.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/bardeen-cooper-schrieffer_-_bcs_-_theory_of_superconductivity.png)\n*Illustration of the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory of superconductivity, highlighting the formation and movement of Cooper pairs.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer theory - Scholarpedia: http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer_theory  \n[2] BCS theory - Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCS_theory  \n[3] Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer Explain Superconductivity - EBSCO: https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/bardeen-cooper-and-schrieffer-explain-superconductivity  \n[4] BCS paper - UMD Physics Department: https://www.physics.umd.edu/courses/Phys798C/AnlageSpring24/BCS%20Paper%20PR%20108%201175_1.pdf  \n[5] Theory of Superconductivity | Phys. Rev.: https://link.aps.org/doi/10.1103/PhysRev.108.1175\n\n## High-Temperature Superconductors\n\nHigh-temperature superconductors (HTS) are materials that exhibit superconductivity at temperatures above 77 K, the boiling point of liquid nitrogen. The discovery of the first HTS in 1986 by Georg Bednorz and K. Alex Müller marked a significant breakthrough in condensed matter physics. Their work on lanthanum barium copper oxide (LBCO) led to the development of yttrium barium copper oxide (YBCO), which has a critical temperature of 93 K, making it the first superconductor to operate above the liquid nitrogen threshold. This advancement opened new avenues for practical applications, as cooling with liquid nitrogen is more cost-effective than using liquid helium, required for traditional superconductors.\n\nHTS materials, primarily ceramics, have unique properties that allow them to maintain superconductivity in higher magnetic fields compared to conventional superconductors. This characteristic is crucial for applications in powerful electromagnets, such as those used in MRI machines and particle accelerators. Ongoing research aims to understand the mechanisms behind HTS and to discover new materials that can operate at even higher temperatures, potentially leading to room-temperature superconductors.\n\n\n\n![A digital illustration of high-temperature superconductivity, highlighting the lattice structure and electron pairing.](https://www.news.harvard.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/hightemp.twist_.jpg)\n*A digital illustration of high-temperature superconductivity, highlighting the lattice structure and electron pairing.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] High-temperature superconductivity: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity  \n[2] Intro to high-temperature superconductors - Magnet Academy: https://nationalmaglab.org/magnet-academy/read-science-stories/science-simplified/high-temperature-superconductors/  \n[3] Investigating High-Temperature Superconductors: https://www.energy.gov/science/articles/investigating-high-temperature-superconductors  \n[4] High-temperature superconductors with a twist: https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2024/01/high-temperature-superconductors-with-a-twist/  \n[5] Milestones: High-Temperature Superconductivity, 1987: https://ethw.org/Milestones:High-Temperature_Superconductivity,_1987\n\n## Applications of Superconductivity\n\nSuperconductors play a crucial role in various technological applications, particularly in medical imaging, power transmission, and transportation.
