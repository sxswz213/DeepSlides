These technologies enable automated analysis of medical images, enhancing diagnostic accuracy and efficiency. CNNs, for instance, excel in feature extraction from images, allowing for precise classification and segmentation of various conditions, including tumors and fractures. \n\nDeep learning models, such as U-Net and vision transformers, have been developed to improve image segmentation and feature extraction, respectively. These models can process vast amounts of data, learning complex patterns that may be overlooked by human experts. Additionally, generative adversarial networks (GANs) are being utilized for image synthesis and augmentation, further enhancing the training datasets available for model development.\n\nDespite these advancements, challenges remain, including data privacy, model interpretability, and the need for integration into clinical workflows. AI algorithms can analyze vast amounts of imaging data quickly, identifying patterns and anomalies that may be missed by human eyes. This capability not only reduces human error but also accelerates the diagnostic process, allowing radiologists to focus on more complex cases and improving patient outcomes overall [1][2][5].\n\nMoreover, AI enhances operational efficiency by streamlining workflows. For instance, AI can automate routine tasks such as image acquisition and interpretation, which is particularly beneficial in high-volume healthcare settings [1][4].
