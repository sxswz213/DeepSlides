This epidemic struck during the Peloponnesian War, leading to the death of an estimated 75,000 to 100,000 people, or about 25% of Athens' population. The disease likely originated in sub-Saharan Africa, spreading through the port of Piraeus into the densely populated city, exacerbated by overcrowding due to wartime strategies. Thucydides documented symptoms such as high fever, vomiting, and severe skin lesions, alongside a profound psychological impact that led to a decline in social order and religious faith.\n\nFollowing the Plague of Athens, the Antonine Plague (165-180 CE) and the Justinian Plague (541-542 CE) further exemplified the devastating effects of pandemics. The Antonine Plague, believed to be caused by smallpox or measles, resulted in approximately 5 million deaths across the Roman Empire. The Justinian Plague, caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*, is estimated to have killed 25-50 million people, reshaping Europe's socio-political landscape. The Black Death (1347-1351) followed, claiming 25 million lives in Europe alone, leading to significant economic and social upheaval, including labor shortages and increased wages for surviving workers.\n\n\n\n![Map illustrating the spread of the Justinian Plague (541-542 A.D.) across the Byzantine and Eastern Roman Empires.](https://image3.slideserve.com/5762928/the-justinian-plague-541-542-a-d-l.jpg)\n*Map illustrating the spread of the Justinian Plague (541-542 A.D.) across the Byzantine and Eastern Roman Empires.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Plague of Athens - The Spanish Flu of 1918, caused by the H1N1 influenza virus, infected approximately one-third of the world's population and resulted in an estimated 50 million deaths. Uniquely, it had a high mortality rate among healthy young adults, a phenomenon attributed to an overactive immune response known as a cytokine storm [1][2].\n\nFollowing this, the HIV/AIDS pandemic emerged in the late 20th century, leading to over 36 million deaths globally. Despite advancements in antiretroviral therapy, HIV remains a significant public health challenge, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa [3][4]. \n\nThe SARS outbreak in 2002-2003 highlighted the rapid spread of coronaviruses, resulting in 774 deaths worldwide. This was a precursor to the COVID-19 pandemic, which began in late 2019 and has since claimed over 7 million lives. COVID-19 has disrupted daily life globally, leading to widespread economic and social changes [1][4][5].\n\nThese pandemics underscore the importance of global health preparedness and the need for coordinated responses to emerging infectious diseases.\n\n\n\n![A timeline illustrating the history and death tolls of major pandemics from the Antonine Plague to COVID-19.](https://specials-images.forbesimg.com/imageserve/5e6fe438e1e61700080ef359/960x0.jpg?fit=scale)\n*A timeline illustrating the history and death tolls of major pandemics from the Antonine Plague to COVID-19.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Pandemic Overview: https://pandemic.internationalsos.com/Overview/Pandemic  \n[2] Pandemics Throughout the History - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8525686/  \n[3] Major Pandemics of the Modern Era | Council on Foreign Relations: https://www.cfr.org/timeline/major-epidemics-modern-era  \n[4] A Tale of Three Recent Pandemics: Influenza, HIV and SARS-CoV-2: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2022.889643/full  \n[5] Societal Impacts of Pandemics: Comparing COVID-19 With History: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8072022/\n\n## Factors Contributing to Pandemics\n\nPandemics arise from a complex interplay of factors, with zoonotic transmission, climate change, and human behavior being the most significant. Zoonotic diseases, which are transmitted from animals to humans, are heavily influenced by environmental changes. For example, climate change alters habitats, affecting wildlife distribution and increasing the likelihood of spillover events. Warmer temperatures can expand the range of disease-carrying vectors, such as mosquitoes, facilitating the spread of diseases like malaria and dengue fever in new areas [1][2].\n\nHuman activities, including deforestation, urbanization, and wildlife trade, bring people into closer contact with wildlife, heightening the risk of zoonotic spillover [3][4]. The COVID-19 pandemic serves as a stark reminder of how human actions can precipitate widespread health crises, highlighting the need for integrated approaches that consider environmental, social, and health factors in pandemic preparedness and response [5]. Lessons from past pandemics underscore the importance of understanding these interconnected factors to effectively mitigate future outbreaks.\n\n\n\n![Connections between sources of emerging zoonotic diseases, including live animal markets and wildlife hunting.](https://www.frontiersin.org/files/Articles/582743/fvets-07-582743-HTML/image_m/fvets-07-582743-g001.jpg)\n*Connections between sources of emerging zoonotic diseases, including live animal markets and wildlife hunting.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Changing climate and the COVID-19 pandemic: more than just heads or tails - Nature: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-021-01303-y  \n[2] Climate Change and Zoonotic Disease Outbreaks - PubMed Central: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12193306/  \n[3] Climate change and contagion: the emerging threat of zoonotic diseases - Taylor & Francis: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/20008686.2024.2441534  \n[4] Climate change and pandemics: a call for action - The Lancet: https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanplh/article/PIIS2542-5196(25)00180-9/fulltext  \n[5] The role of climatic changes in the emergence and re-emergence of infectious diseases: bibliometric analysis and literature-supported studies on zoonoses - One Health Outlook: https://onehealthoutlook.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s42522-024-00127-3\n\n# Global History of Pandemics\n\n## Introduction\nThe history of pandemics reveals critical lessons about the interplay between infectious diseases and human societies.
