The United Nations Conference on the Human Environment in Stockholm in 1972 was a pivotal moment, establishing the first international framework for environmental protection. The Stockholm Declaration recognized the right to a healthy environment and laid the groundwork for future agreements, including the establishment of the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) [1][5].\n\nFollowing Stockholm, the 1992 Rio Earth Summit further advanced international environmental law by introducing key documents such as the Rio Declaration and the Convention on Biological Diversity. These agreements emphasized sustainable development and the need for international cooperation to address global environmental challenges [2][3]. The Kyoto Protocol in 1997 and the Paris Agreement in 2016 marked further progress, focusing on climate change and binding commitments from nations to reduce greenhouse gas emissions [4].\n\nToday, environmental law continues to evolve, integrating with human rights and economic policies, reflecting a growing recognition of the interconnectedness of environmental issues with global governance [4][5].\n\n\n\n![Timeline of significant environmental events from 1970 to 1972, including the establishment of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and the U.N. The increasing frequency of extreme weather events and rising global temperatures exacerbate these issues, making it difficult for existing legal frameworks to adapt.
