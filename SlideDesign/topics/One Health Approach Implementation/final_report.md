It emphasizes that the health of each component is interdependent, and effective health strategies must consider all three domains simultaneously. This concept has evolved over time, with roots tracing back to the early 19th century when scientists began to understand the links between human and animal diseases, particularly zoonoses—diseases that can be transmitted between animals and humans.\n\nHistorically, the term \"One Medicine\" was coined by Dr. Calvin Schwabe in the 20th century, advocating for a unified approach to health that integrates veterinary and human medicine. The modern term \"One Health\" gained traction in the early 2000s, particularly after the emergence of infectious diseases like SARS and H1N1, which highlighted the need for a coordinated response to health threats at the human-animal-environment interface. This holistic framework recognizes that the health of people is closely linked to the health of animals and the environment, necessitating collaboration across various sectors to address health challenges effectively. \n\nHuman health is often impacted by zoonotic diseases, which are diseases that can be transmitted between animals and humans. For instance, the COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the importance of understanding these connections and the need for integrated surveillance systems that monitor health threats across species. Animal health is equally crucial, as healthy animals contribute to food security and economic stability, while environmental health is vital for sustaining ecosystems that support both human and animal life.\n\nThe One Health approach promotes communication, collaboration, and coordination among diverse stakeholders, including healthcare providers, veterinarians, ecologists, and policymakers. One successful initiative is the National One Health Framework to Address Zoonotic Diseases and Advance Public Health Preparedness in the United States, which aims to enhance coordination among federal agencies like the CDC, USDA, and DOI. Additionally, local initiatives, such as those in Turkmenistan, demonstrate how national policies can be effectively translated into practice, showcasing the importance of tailored strategies that consider regional contexts[4].\n\nThese frameworks and initiatives illustrate the potential of the One Health approach to create sustainable health outcomes through collaborative efforts.\n\n\n\n![Five steps for implementing the One Health approach, including situation analysis, governance setup, planning, implementation, and review.](https://cdn.unenvironment.org/s3fs-public/inline-images/One-Helath.png)\n*Five steps for implementing the One Health approach, including situation analysis, governance setup, planning, implementation, and review.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Implementation framework for One Health approach - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6607818/  \n[2] One Health - World Health Organization (WHO): https://www.who.int/europe/initiatives/one-health  \n[3] National ONE HEALTH Framework to Address Zoonotic Diseases and Advance Public Health Preparedness in the United States: https://www.cdc.gov/one-health/media/pdfs/2025/01/354391-A-NOHF-ZOONOSES-508_FINAL.pdf  \n[4] U.S. Government Releases First National One Health Plan to Protect People, Animals, and Our Environment from Shared Health Threats: https://www.cdc.gov/media/releases/2025/us-government-releases-national-one-health-plan.html  \n[5] National One Health Framework to Address Zoonotic Diseases and Advance Public Health Preparedness: https://www.naccho.org/blog/articles/national-one-health-framework-2025-2029\n\n## Challenges in One Health Implementation\n\nThe implementation of the One Health approach faces several significant challenges.
