Continued advancements in technology and methodologies will enhance the efficacy and accessibility of these diagnostic tools, ultimately improving patient outcomes and healthcare delivery.\n\n## Overview of CRISPR Technology\n\nCRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) technology is a revolutionary tool in molecular biology, primarily known for its gene-editing capabilities. It consists of two main components: the Cas (CRISPR-associated) proteins and guide RNA (gRNA). The most widely used Cas proteins include Cas9, Cas12, and Cas13, each with unique functions and applications in diagnostics.\n\nCas9, a Class 2, Type II system, is primarily used for DNA editing, creating double-strand breaks at specific genomic locations. Cas12, a Type V system, offers additional trans-cleavage activity, allowing it to cut nearby single-stranded DNA after targeting its specific sequence. This feature enhances its utility in diagnostic platforms like DETECTR. Cas13, a Type VI system, targets RNA instead of DNA, making it suitable for RNA detection and manipulation, as seen in the SHERLOCK platform.\n\nThe CRISPR/Cas systems operate through three stages: adaptation, expression, and interference. During adaptation, foreign DNA is integrated into the CRISPR array, while expression involves the transcription of this array into crRNA. Finally, during interference, the Cas proteins, guided by crRNA, cleave the target nucleic acids, providing a defense mechanism against invaders. This technology has transformed diagnostics by enabling rapid, sensitive, and specific detection of pathogens and genetic mutations, marking a significant advancement in molecular diagnostics.\n\n\n\n![Mechanisms of CRISPR-associated proteins Cas9, Cas12a, Cas12f, and Cas13a.](https://pubs.rsc.org/image/article/2021/sc/d0sc06973f/d0sc06973f-f1_hi-res.gif)\n*Mechanisms of CRISPR-associated proteins Cas9, Cas12a, Cas12f, and Cas13a.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] CRISPR-Cas systems in diagnostics: A comprehensive ...: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666388022000090  \n[2] State of the art CRISPR-based strategies for cancer ...: https://biomarkerres.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s40364-024-00701-x  \n[3] CRISPR Technology: Mechanisms and Applications in ...: https://www.intechopen.com/chapters/1209603  \n[4] Advances in the application of CRISPR-Cas technology ...: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/molecular-biosciences/articles/10.3389/fmolb.2023.1260883/full  \n[5] CRISPR/Cas-based nucleic acid detection strategies ...: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405844024022102\n\n## Types of CRISPR Diagnostic Platforms\n\nCRISPR diagnostic platforms have emerged as innovative tools for disease detection, leveraging the precision of CRISPR technology. Two prominent platforms are SHERLOCK (Specific High-sensitivity Enzymatic Reporter unlocking) and DETECTR (DNA Endonuclease Targeted CRISPR Trans Reporter). SHERLOCK utilizes the Cas13 protein, while DETECTR employs Cas12a, both of which enable highly specific detection of nucleic acids through collateral cleavage activity, generating detectable signals upon target recognition [1][2].\n\nSHERLOCK has been particularly noted for its ability to detect multiple targets simultaneously and its user-friendly paper-based format, which allows results to be read without specialized equipment [4]. DETECTR, on the other hand, is known for its rapid response times and high sensitivity, making it suitable for point-of-care testing [2][5]. Both platforms have been adapted for various applications, including the detection of infectious diseases like SARS-CoV-2 and cancer biomarkers in circulating tumor DNA [1][3].\n\nIn addition to SHERLOCK and DETECTR, paper-based systems are gaining traction due to their portability and ease of use, making them ideal for resource-limited settings [1][5]. The primary CRISPR systems used in diagnostics are Cas9, Cas12, and Cas13, each with unique properties that influence their sensitivity, specificity, cost-effectiveness, and ease of use.\n\nCas9 is widely recognized for its high specificity and sensitivity, making it suitable for detecting genetic mutations and pathogens. However, it often requires complex laboratory setups, which can limit its use in point-of-care (POC) settings. In contrast, Cas12 and Cas13 offer advantages in rapid testing and multiplexing capabilities, allowing for the simultaneous detection of multiple targets. These systems are particularly beneficial in resource-limited environments due to their lower infrastructure requirements and cost-effectiveness [1][2].\n\nRecent advancements have integrated CRISPR diagnostics with biosensing technologies, enhancing their accessibility and usability. For instance, CRISPR-based tests can now be performed with minimal equipment, making them ideal for POC applications [3][4].
