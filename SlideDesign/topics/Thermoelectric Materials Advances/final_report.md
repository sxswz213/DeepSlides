This phenomenon occurs when a temperature gradient across a conductor generates an electric potential, which is quantified by the Seebeck coefficient (S). The efficiency of thermoelectric materials is often evaluated using the dimensionless figure of merit, ZT, defined as:\n\n\\[ ZT = \\frac{S^2 \\sigma T}{\\kappa} \\]\n\nwhere \\( \\sigma \\) is the electrical conductivity, \\( T \\) is the absolute temperature, and \\( \\kappa \\) is the thermal conductivity. A higher ZT value indicates better performance, as it reflects a material's ability to generate electricity from heat.\n\nThe Seebeck effect is reversible, leading to the Peltier effect, where an electric current can create a temperature difference. This dual functionality makes thermoelectric materials valuable for both power generation and refrigeration applications. Research continues to focus on optimizing these materials to enhance their thermoelectric properties, particularly by improving their ZT values through material engineering and doping strategies.\n\n\n\n![Diagram showing the relationships between the Seebeck coefficient, Peltier coefficient, electrical conductance, thermal conductance, and the thermoelectric figure of merit (ZT).](https://engineering.purdue.edu/gekcogrp/science-applications/thermo/thermo_img/thermo_fig2.JPG)\n*Diagram showing the relationships between the Seebeck coefficient, Peltier coefficient, electrical conductance, thermal conductance, and the thermoelectric figure of merit (ZT).*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Relationship between thermoelectric figure of merit and energy ... - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4500231/  \n[2] Thermoelectric Materials for Energy - Number Analytics - https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/thermoelectric-materials-energy-guide  \n[3] Thermoelectric materials - Wikipedia - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoelectric_materials  \n[4] ZT – figure of merit - Linseis - https://www.linseis.com/en/properties/zt-figure-of-merit/  \n[5] Thermoelectric Properties of Materials - https://thermoelectrics.matsci.northwestern.edu/thermoelectrics/index.html\n\n## Recent Advances in Thermoelectric Materials\n\nRecent developments in thermoelectric materials have significantly advanced their applications, particularly in biomedicine. By integrating carbon nanotubes (CNTs) with transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) such as WS2 and MoS2, researchers have developed advanced materials that exhibit improved thermoelectric properties. For instance, a study demonstrated that blending multiwalled carbon nanotubes with TMDs significantly enhances the electrical conductivity and thermoelectric efficiency of the resulting nanocomposites, making them suitable for applications in wearable electronics and energy harvesting systems [1][3].\n\nMoreover, the incorporation of elastomers into these nanocomposites has led to the creation of stretchable thermoelectric films. These films maintain high performance even under mechanical strain, showcasing a power factor of 372.79 μW m−1 K−2 at 50% strain, which is a remarkable achievement for flexible thermoelectric materials [2]. The versatility of nanocomposites, particularly those combining CNTs and TMDs, opens new avenues for developing efficient thermoelectric devices that can adapt to various applications.\n\n\n\n![Schematic representation of 3D nanocomposites formed from surfactant-functionalized TMDs and MWNTs.](https://pub.mdpi-res.com/materials/materials-14-00896/article_deploy/html/images/materials-14-00896-ag.png?1614397463)\n*Schematic representation of 3D nanocomposites formed from surfactant-functionalized TMDs and MWNTs.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Nanocomposites Prepared from Carbon Nanotubes and the ...: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7918728/  \n[2] High-performance ladder-type conjugated polymer/carbon nanotube ...: https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2024/tc/d4tc01307g  \n[3] Advances and Outlooks for Carbon Nanotube‐Based ...: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11962713/\n\n## Low-Temperature Thermoelectric Generators\n\nLow-temperature thermoelectric generators (TEGs) are emerging as vital components in biomedical applications, particularly for energy harvesting from body heat. One of the primary issues is the low efficiency of current thermoelectric devices, which limits their broader application. To enhance performance, researchers are focusing on improving the figure of merit, *zT*, which is crucial for achieving competitive efficiency with existing technologies. Materials with *zT* values greater than 2.5 have been recorded, but achieving a *zT* of at least 4 is necessary to rival conventional systems like Rankine cycles [1][2].\n\nAnother challenge is the sustainability of thermoelectric materials. Many traditional materials, such as Bi2Te3 and PbTe, raise concerns regarding their long-term availability and toxicity [1]. Future research must prioritize the discovery of abundant, non-toxic materials that can be processed at lower temperatures. This includes exploring new classes of materials, such as organics and 2D materials, which may offer promising alternatives [1][3].\n\nMoreover, optimizing interfaces and ensuring long-term stability in thermoelectric devices are critical for their practical application. Addressing these challenges will pave the way for advancements in energy conversion technologies, contributing to global sustainability goals [2][4].\n\n\n\n![Illustration depicting various applications of thermoelectric materials and associated challenges.](https://labdata.labapress.com/labapress-data/matlab/newcreate/MATLAB-2023-0032-Abstract.jpg)\n*Illustration depicting various applications of thermoelectric materials and associated challenges.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Thermoelectric Materials: Current Status and Future Challenges: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/electronic-materials/articles/10.3389/femat.2021.677845/full  \n[2] Advances and Challenges in SnTe‐Based Thermoelectrics: https://advanced.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/adma.202418280  \n[3] Ionic thermoelectric materials: Innovations and challenges: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2542529324000518  \n[4] Challenges and perspective recent trends of enhancing the ...: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2352492823017749\n\n# Thermoelectric Materials Advances\n\nThermoelectric materials have garnered significant attention due to their ability to convert temperature differences into electrical energy, offering sustainable solutions for energy harvesting and cooling applications.
