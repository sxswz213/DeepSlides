The concept of GIS began in the early 1960s with Roger Tomlinson, who developed the first GIS for the Canada Land Inventory, laying the groundwork for modern spatial analysis [1][5]. This system allowed for the digitization of maps and the integration of various data layers, revolutionizing land management practices.\n\nIn the 1970s, the launch of the first Landsat satellite provided critical remote sensing data, enhancing GIS capabilities by allowing users to analyze real-time geographic information [3][4]. The 1980s saw the emergence of commercial GIS software, notably Esri's ArcGIS, which became a market leader and expanded GIS applications across various sectors, including urban planning and environmental management [2][3].\n\nThe late 1990s marked a pivotal shift with the rise of web-based GIS, enabling data sharing and interactive mapping through web browsers [3]. One primary approach involves the digitization and georeferencing of historical maps, allowing researchers to overlay these maps with contemporary data. This technique enables the comparison of geographical changes over time, enhancing our understanding of historical contexts [4].\n\nData collection in HGIS often includes historical microdata, such as census records, which can be spatially analyzed to reveal patterns and trends. Techniques like algorithmic interpolation help estimate missing data, while spatial correlation methods identify clusters and relationships within the data [3][5]. Additionally, advanced spatial statistics are increasingly employed to explore complex historical phenomena, moving beyond simple visualizations to more nuanced analyses [2].\n\nThe integration of these methodologies not only enriches historical research but also facilitates interdisciplinary collaboration, as scholars from various fields apply GIS tools to their inquiries [1][3].
