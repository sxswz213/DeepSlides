This route facilitated trade between China, Southeast Asia, India, the Middle East, and eventually Europe. As shipbuilding technology advanced, merchants could navigate longer distances, enhancing trade efficiency and cultural exchange. Goods such as silk, spices, and precious metals were transported along these routes, while ideas, religions, and technologies spread among diverse cultures.\n\nInitially, Austronesian sailors dominated trade in Southeast Asia, utilizing advanced ship designs. Persian and Arab traders played significant roles, particularly in the Arabian Sea. By the 10th century, Chinese ships began to participate actively in these trade routes, further expanding the network. The Maritime Silk Road not only served as a conduit for commerce but also fostered cultural interactions, shaping the civilizations along its routes and contributing to pre-modern globalization.
