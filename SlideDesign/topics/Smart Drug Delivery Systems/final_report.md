Among the most prominent types are nanoparticles, liposomes, micelles, and hydrogels.\n\nNanoparticles, typically ranging from 1 to 100 nanometers, are engineered to improve drug solubility and stability while enabling targeted delivery to specific tissues. They can be functionalized with ligands for enhanced specificity, making them particularly effective in cancer therapy by exploiting the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect [[2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666845925001163)][[4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11040566/)].\n\nLiposomes are spherical vesicles that encapsulate drugs, providing a biocompatible and biodegradable delivery method. They can be designed to release their contents in response to specific stimuli, such as pH changes or light [[4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11040566/)].\n\nMicelles, formed from amphiphilic molecules, can solubilize hydrophobic drugs and enhance their bioavailability. They also respond to environmental triggers, allowing for controlled drug release [[4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11040566/)].\n\nHydrogels are three-dimensional networks that can swell in response to environmental stimuli, enabling localized and sustained drug release. Passive targeting relies on the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, which allows nanoparticles to accumulate in tumor tissues due to their leaky vasculature. This method is effective for delivering drugs without the need for specific targeting ligands, making it a straightforward approach for many cancer therapies [1][2].\n\nIn contrast, active targeting involves the use of ligands that specifically bind to receptors overexpressed on cancer cells. This strategy enhances the delivery of therapeutic agents directly to the tumor site, significantly increasing the concentration of the drug at the target while minimizing exposure to healthy tissues [3][4]. \n\nAdditionally, SDDS can incorporate stimuli-responsive materials that release drugs in response to specific environmental triggers, such as pH changes or temperature variations. This feature allows for controlled drug release, ensuring that therapeutic agents are delivered precisely when and where they are needed [5]. \n\nOverall, the combination of passive and active targeting, along with stimuli-responsive mechanisms, enhances the efficacy and safety of drug delivery in cancer treatment.\n\n\n\n![Mechanism of passive and active targeting in drug delivery systems.](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/351614741/figure/fig3/AS:1024036859568130@1621160821664/Mechanism-of-passive-targeting-and-active-targeting-93.png)\n*Mechanism of passive and active targeting in drug delivery systems.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Smart drug delivery systems for precise cancer therapy - PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9643298/  \n[2] Smart nanocarrier-based drug delivery systems for cancer therapy: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2090123218300791  \n[3] An overview of active and passive targeting strategies to improve the: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jphp.13098  \n[4] Recent Advances in Targeted Drug Delivery Strategy for Enhancing: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10534854/  \n[5] “Smart” drug delivery: A window to future of translational medicine: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/chemistry/articles/10.3389/fchem.2022.1095598/full\n\n## Clinical Applications and Future Directions\n\nSmart Drug Delivery Systems (SDDS) are revolutionizing cancer treatment by enhancing the precision and efficacy of drug delivery.
