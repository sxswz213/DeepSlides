It consists of five key stages: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test. Each stage plays a crucial role in developing innovative solutions, particularly in public policy.\n\n1. **Empathize**: This initial stage involves engaging with users to gain insights into their experiences and challenges. It requires active listening and observation to understand their needs deeply.\n\n2. **Define**: In this phase, the insights gathered are synthesized to articulate a clear problem statement. This statement should focus on user needs rather than organizational goals, guiding the ideation process.\n\n3. **Ideate**: Here, teams brainstorm a wide range of ideas and solutions. The goal is to encourage creativity and explore various possibilities without immediate judgment.\n\n4. **Prototype**: This stage involves creating tangible representations of ideas. Prototypes can be low-fidelity models that allow for quick testing and feedback, helping to refine concepts.\n\n5. **Test**: The final stage focuses on evaluating prototypes with real users. Feedback is gathered to identify areas for improvement, leading to further iterations of the design.\n\nThese stages are not strictly linear; they often overlap and inform one another, fostering a flexible and iterative approach to problem-solving in public policy contexts. By applying design thinking, policymakers can create more effective, user-centered solutions that address the complexities of societal issues. This methodology encourages collaboration and co-creation, ensuring that diverse perspectives are considered throughout the process.\n\n\n\n![The five phases of design thinking: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test.](https://www.aihr.com/wp-content/uploads/5-Phases-of-Design-Thinking-social.png)\n*The five phases of design thinking: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] How is design thinking shaping innovative public policies?
