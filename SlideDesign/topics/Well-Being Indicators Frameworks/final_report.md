One of the most pivotal moments was the 2009 Stiglitz-Sen-Fitoussi Report, which emphasized the inadequacy of GDP as a measure of societal progress and called for a broader understanding of well-being that includes social, economic, and environmental dimensions. This report catalyzed various global efforts to develop comprehensive well-being frameworks, notably the OECD's Better Life Initiative, which aims to provide a multidimensional perspective on well-being by integrating various indicators such as health, education, and environmental quality.\n\nIn Australia, frameworks have evolved to address both Indigenous and non-Indigenous populations, reflecting the need for culturally relevant measures. The Australian Bureau of Statistics has developed several frameworks, including the socio-economic well-being framework, which focuses on economic resources and social conditions. However, these frameworks often overlook the intrinsic connection Indigenous peoples have with their land, or \"Country,\" which is vital for their well-being. These frameworks often incorporate a mix of objective and subjective indicators, reflecting diverse methodologies and objectives. For instance, the OECD emphasizes a comprehensive approach that includes health, income, education, and environmental factors, aiming to provide a holistic view of well-being across its member countries [1].\n\nThe United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) introduced the Human Development Index (HDI), which ranks countries based on health, education, and income, highlighting the importance of human capabilities in development [4]. Similarly, the OECD's Better Life Index allows individuals to weigh different aspects of well-being according to personal values, thus promoting a more tailored understanding of quality of life [5].\n\nAdditionally, frameworks like the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) challenge traditional economic measures by incorporating social and environmental factors, offering a more nuanced perspective on societal well-being [3]. Various frameworks share core principles that promote a comprehensive view of well-being, integrating economic, social, environmental, and health dimensions. This multifaceted approach acknowledges the complexity of human experiences.\n\nA key principle is the inclusion of subjective well-being, which captures personal satisfaction and happiness alongside objective measures like income and education. The OECD framework exemplifies this integration, advocating for a blend of subjective indicators with traditional economic metrics to provide a holistic understanding of well-being [4]. \n\nMoreover, many frameworks highlight the interconnectedness of well-being domains. For instance, the Australian Government's \"Measuring What Matters\" initiative aligns indicators across social, economic, and environmental dimensions, recognizing that improvements in one area can positively impact others [1]. \n\nUltimately, these frameworks aim to inform policies that enhance overall well-being, ensuring diverse perspectives and needs are considered in decision-making processes. A primary issue is the disconnect between framework development and practical application. For example, a Gallup study revealed that only 34% of employees felt engaged at work, highlighting a gap between organizational initiatives and employee experiences [1]. Additionally, resistance to change is prevalent; many employees express skepticism about the authenticity of well-being programs. In one Fortune 500 company, only 25% of employees participated in a new wellness initiative due to trust issues [1].\n\nMoreover, defining and measuring well-being indicators is complex. Different regions may require tailored approaches, making it unrealistic to apply identical indicators universally [4]. Barriers such as busy work environments, financial constraints, and inadequate organizational support further complicate implementation [2][4]. To overcome these challenges, organizations can adopt participatory approaches, involving employees in the design of well-being initiatives, which has been shown to significantly increase engagement rates [1]. By fostering transparency and genuine leadership commitment, companies can create a culture that prioritizes well-being and enhances participation.\n\n\n\n![Factors influencing the creation of a health and well-being culture in organizations.](https://www.england.nhs.uk/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/Create-a-HWB-Culture-724x1024.png)\n*Factors influencing the creation of a health and well-being culture in organizations.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] Integrating Employee WellBeing into Performance Measurement Frameworks: https://blogs.psico-smart.com/blog-integrating-employee-wellbeing-into-performance-measurement-frameworks-166943  \n[2] Barriers and facilitators to implementing workplace health and wellbeing services in the NHS: https://bmcpublichealth.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12889-018-6283-y  \n[3] Challenges in Constructing a Survey-Based Well-Being Framework: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5901737/  \n[4] Wellbeing Frameworks: Emerging Practice, Challenges, and Opportunities: https://academic.oup.com/book/46095/chapter/404620010\n\n# Well-Being Indicators Frameworks\n\n## Introduction\nThe report on Well-Being Indicators Frameworks aims to explore the evolution, implementation, and challenges of various frameworks designed to assess and enhance quality of life.
