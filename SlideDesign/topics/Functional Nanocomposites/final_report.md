These nanomaterials can be categorized into three main types: nanocarbons, metal oxides, and polymers. \n\nNanocarbons, such as carbon nanotubes and graphene, are known for their exceptional mechanical strength and electrical conductivity. They are widely used in applications ranging from electronics to structural materials. Metal oxides, like titanium dioxide and zinc oxide, are often utilized for their photocatalytic properties and ability to improve thermal stability in polymer matrices. Functionalized metal oxides can also impart antibacterial properties, making them suitable for biomedical applications [1][5].\n\nPolymers, when combined with nanomaterials, form polymer nanocomposites that exhibit improved mechanical, thermal, and barrier properties. These composites are increasingly used in packaging, automotive, and aerospace industries due to their lightweight and durable nature [2][4]. Self-assembly involves the spontaneous organization of molecules into structured arrangements, often driven by non-covalent interactions. For instance, a study demonstrated the synthesis of a multifunctional Fe3O4@SiO2@PEI-Au/Ag@PDA nanocomposite through a self-assembled method, where magnetic nanoparticles were coated with silica and then functionalized with gold and silver nanoparticles using electrostatic interactions and dopamine reduction. This method not only enhances catalytic activity but also allows for easy recovery using magnetic fields, making it highly efficient for practical applications in catalysis and environmental remediation[1].\n\nSurface modification techniques, such as chemical grafting and polymer blending, are also crucial in tailoring the properties of nanocomposites. These methods improve compatibility and functionality, enabling the incorporation of various active components into the nanocomposite matrix.
