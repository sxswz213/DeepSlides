It encompasses a wide range of activities, including the production, dissemination, and consumption of the arts, as well as the preservation of cultural heritage. Historically, cultural policy has evolved from a focus on elite art forms to a broader approach that includes community and amateur cultural activities, reflecting changing societal values and priorities. This shift has been particularly evident since the 1970s, as policies began to emphasize cultural identity and the valorization of indigenous cultures, moving beyond traditional Western art forms to include diverse cultural expressions.\n\nThe formulation of cultural policy involves various stakeholders, including government bodies, cultural organizations, and the public. Governments play a crucial role in implementing these policies through funding, regulations, and the establishment of cultural institutions. The evolution of cultural policy is influenced by factors such as technological advancements, economic considerations, and shifts in societal values, particularly regarding inclusivity and diversity in cultural expression. The region's focus on supporting creative industries has led to a notable increase in cultural exports, which doubled in recent years, highlighting the economic potential of cultural investments [2][3].\n\nConversely, challenges arise when cultural policies prioritize economic outcomes over equitable access to the arts.
