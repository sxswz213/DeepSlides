Left-wing populism advocates for social justice, economic equality, and the empowerment of marginalized groups, often criticizing corporate power and the political establishment for perpetuating inequality. In contrast, right-wing populism emphasizes nationalism, viewing immigrants and foreign influences as threats to national identity and security. This form typically attracts support from segments of the working class who feel marginalized by globalization and cultural shifts [1][4][5].\n\nDespite their shared disdain for elites, left-wing and right-wing populists define \"the people\" differently. Left-wing populists envision a broad coalition of diverse citizens, while right-wing populists often adopt a more exclusionary definition based on ethnicity or nationality [2][3][5]. This distinction influences their political strategies and electoral bases, leading to varied implications for democratic processes and societal cohesion. Populist leaders typically challenge established norms, viewing institutions like the judiciary and media as obstacles to their agenda. This perspective can result in the undermining of judicial independence, as seen in countries like Hungary and Poland, where reforms have been implemented to exert control over the judiciary and limit its power [1][2].\n\nMoreover, populism tends to concentrate power within the executive branch, reducing legislative oversight and accountability. This centralization often manifests through executive orders that bypass traditional legislative processes, as observed in Turkey under President Erdoğan [2][3]. The consequences of such actions include diminished civil liberties, with reports indicating a decline in freedom of the press and political rights under populist regimes [1][4].\n\nWhile populism can mobilize previously disengaged citizens and highlight critical social issues, its overall impact on democratic institutions is concerning. The challenge lies in balancing the responsiveness to popular demands with the preservation of democratic norms and values [2][5].\n\n\n\n![Diagram illustrating the checks and balances within democratic institutions.](https://www.wallstreetmojo.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Picture71.jpg.webp)\n*Diagram illustrating the checks and balances within democratic institutions.*\n\n\n### Sources\n[1] The Populist Harm to Democracy: An Empirical Assessment: https://institute.global/insights/geopolitics-and-security/populist-harm-democracy-empirical-assessment  \n[2] THE IMPACT OF POPULISM ON DEMOCRATIC INSTITUTIONS: https://www.ijrar.org/papers/IJRAR19D5433.pdf  \n[3] Populism and the Erosion of Democratic Checks and Balances: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/384839790_Populism_and_the_Erosion_of_Democratic_Checks_and_Balances_A_Systematic_Literature_Review_across_Regions  \n[4] Conditions and consequences of populism and democratic backsliding: https://www.idea.int/sites/default/files/publications/chapters/conditions-and-consequences-of-populism-and-democratic-backsliding.pdf  \n[5] \"THE RISE OF POPULISM AND ITS IMPACT ON DEMOCRATIC INSTITUTIONS\": https://www.granthaalayahpublication.org/Arts-Journal/ShodhKosh/article/download/4423/3997/24203\n\n## Case Studies of Populism and Democratic Erosion\n\nPopulism has significantly impacted democratic institutions in various countries, notably Hungary and the United States. In established democracies, such as those in Western Europe and North America, populist movements often challenge political norms without completely dismantling democratic institutions. Leaders like Donald Trump in the U.S. and Marine Le Pen in France have capitalized on economic discontent and cultural anxieties, yet they encounter resistance from strong institutional frameworks and civil society [1][2].\n\nIn contrast, in emerging democracies or those with weaker institutions, populism can lead to severe democratic erosion. Countries like Hungary and Venezuela illustrate this trend, where populist leaders have systematically undermined judicial independence and media freedom, consolidating power at the expense of democratic checks and balances [1][3]. This divergence underscores the importance of institutional resilience in moderating populism's impact. While populism can enhance political engagement by addressing legitimate grievances, its tendency to vilify elites and undermine pluralism poses significant risks to democratic integrity [2][4].
