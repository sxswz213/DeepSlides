One of the most significant issues is leakage current, which occurs when electrons tunnel through thin insulating layers. This phenomenon becomes more pronounced as transistors shrink below 20 nanometers, leading to increased power consumption and reduced efficiency [1][2].\n\nThermal noise is another critical challenge. As transistors become smaller, they generate more heat per unit area, complicating thermal management. This heat can lead to performance degradation and increased failure rates [4][5]. Additionally, quantum effects, such as quantum tunneling, become unavoidable at these scales, further complicating device behavior and reliability [2][3]. \n\nTo address these challenges, researchers are exploring new materials and architectures, such as graphene and carbon nanotubes, which may offer improved electrical properties and thermal management capabilities [1][4]. One promising area is the use of **quantum dots**, which are semiconductor particles that exhibit unique optical and electronic properties due to their nanoscale size. These properties make quantum dots ideal for applications in solar cells, light-emitting diodes (LEDs), and biomedical imaging, where they can enhance efficiency and performance significantly [1][5].\n\nAnother exciting avenue is **molecular electronics**, which aims to utilize individual molecules as electronic components. This approach can potentially replace conventional silicon-based devices, allowing for smaller, more efficient circuits. Molecular switches and wires are being developed to create devices that can operate at the molecular level, overcoming the limitations of traditional fabrication methods [5].\n\nAdditionally, **graphene** and other two-dimensional materials are being investigated for their exceptional electrical, thermal, and mechanical properties. Graphene-based transistors have shown promise in achieving higher performance than silicon counterparts, making them suitable for next-generation electronic devices [1][4].
