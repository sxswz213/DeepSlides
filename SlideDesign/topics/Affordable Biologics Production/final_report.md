This shift not only streamlines workflows but also minimizes the risk of cross-contamination, a critical concern in biopharmaceutical production.\n\nThe adoption of SUT has been driven by the increasing demand for rapid and flexible production capabilities, especially highlighted during the COVID-19 pandemic. Manufacturers can quickly scale operations to meet urgent needs without compromising quality. For instance, single-use bioreactors allow for smaller batch sizes, facilitating the development of personalized therapies and vaccines tailored to specific patient needs.\n\nMoreover, SUT contribute to sustainability efforts by reducing water and energy consumption associated with cleaning processes. These products are designed to be highly similar to existing biologics, offering a cost-effective alternative once the original product's patent expires. The introduction of biosimilars fosters price competition, which can lead to lower costs for patients and healthcare systems without compromising quality, efficacy, or safety [1][4].\n\nRegulatory frameworks play a crucial role in the development and approval of biosimilars. Agencies like the FDA and EMA require a comprehensive \"totality of the evidence\" approach to demonstrate biosimilarity. This includes rigorous analytical studies, non-clinical assessments, and clinical trials focused on one indication, which can then be extrapolated to other indications [2][5]. Such regulatory considerations are essential for ensuring that biosimilars can enter the market efficiently, ultimately enhancing patient access to affordable treatments [3].\n\nThe impact of biosimilars extends beyond individual patients; they contribute to overall healthcare sustainability by reducing the financial burden associated with biologic therapies.
